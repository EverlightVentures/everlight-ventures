# Chapter 1: A World in Conflict

---

## Chapter 1, Part 1: Dust and Badges

The dead horse had been lying in front of the general store since Tuesday.

Nobody moved it. Nobody talked about it. The flies did their work in the heat, and the smell drifted down Main Street like a rumor nobody wanted to confirm, and the people of Sable Flats walked past it with their eyes fixed on the middle distance, the way you walk past something that frightens you more than it should. It was just a horse. Animals died out here all the time. But the way it had gone down, standing one moment, then dropping like its legs had been cut from under it, eyes rolling white, no sound at all, that was the part people did not want to think about.

Hailey Pink thought about it. She thought about it every time she passed the bloated carcass on her afternoon patrol, which was three times now. She thought about the silence of its dying, and she thought about the silence that had settled over the town in the weeks since, and she thought about how the two silences were not really different at all.

She adjusted her hat against the glaring sun and squinted down the length of the street. Late afternoon, and the town should have been stirring, men coming out of the mines, women calling children home for supper, the saloon doors swinging open with laughter and piano music spilling out into the heat. Instead, Sable Flats lay still. A stray dog nosed at the shadow of a water trough but did not drink. A distant wind kicked up whirlwinds of red dirt that danced across the road and collapsed against the boardwalk like exhausted things. Even the horses tied outside the feed store stood motionless, heads hanging, tails limp, their flanks dark with sweat that would not dry.

It was a town like any other in the West, decaying under the weight of time and hardship. The buildings that had once been painted bright reds and blues were now chipped and faded, sun-beaten relics of a more prosperous era. But this was something beyond decay. Decay was slow and honest. This was something else. This was a town holding its breath.

Hailey sighed, her hand drifting to the badge pinned to her chest. She pressed her thumb against it, a reflex she had developed over the past year, a way of reminding herself that the metal was real, that her authority was real, even when everything else felt like it was dissolving. She was twenty-two years old, wild at heart, full of ideas she kept clamped behind her teeth because nobody wanted to hear them. Stubborn as hell. That was what her mother used to say, half with pride and half with warning: *That girl is stubborn as hell, and one day it will either save her or kill her.*

Being a deputy in this town was like trying to hold water in your fists. Every day, she fought against the assumptions of the men around her, men who had long decided that her place was anywhere but where she stood now. The badge was supposed to change that. Some days it did. Most days it was just a piece of tin that caught the light.

She continued down the street, her boots raising small clouds of dust that hung in the air behind her like ghosts of her passing. Past the barber shop with its faded pole, paint peeling in long curls. Past the church, its whitewash gone gray, its bell tower listing slightly to the east as though even the building were trying to lean away from something. Past the boarding house on the corner of Tanner Street, where old Mrs. Cooper lived, the one the townspeople called a healer when they needed her and a witch when they did not. A thin thread of woodsmoke rose from the chimney despite the heat, and through the open window Hailey caught the scent of something herbal and bitter, sage maybe, or something older. Candles burned in two of the windows, their flames steady and pale against the afternoon sun, keeping watch over something Hailey could not name.

She filed it away and kept walking.

Near the saloon, she glanced over her shoulder. Its doors creaked open, and two men shuffled out into the glare, shoulders slumped, eyes dull. There was something off about the way they moved, like their bodies carried a weight that had nothing to do with muscle or bone. One of them stumbled, catching himself on the railing, but neither spoke. Their faces were blank, drained of whatever had once made them men who drank and laughed and argued. It was like they were not really seeing the world around them. Like something behind their eyes had been turned down, the way you turn down a lamp.

The feeling of unease settled deeper in Hailey's bones. She had been noticing more and more people moving that way. Slower. More lethargic. As if some unseen force was sapping the vitality right out of them, the way a drought saps a river, not all at once, but steadily, relentlessly, until one day you look and the riverbed is just cracked mud and the memory of water. To the others, it was just the heat, the hard life out West. Nothing new. But Hailey had watched her mother die of something the doctors could not name, and she knew what the early stages of that kind of dying looked like. It looked exactly like this.

She continued past the boarded-up windows of the old assay office, past the telegraph pole where someone had nailed a notice about a missing child three weeks ago and nobody had taken it down. The paper was bleached nearly white by the sun, the name almost illegible. She did not stop to read it. She had read it before. The child had not been found.

It was late afternoon, but it could have been midnight for how still the town felt. Even the flies seemed sluggish, circling the dead horse in slow, uncertain orbits, as though they too were losing the will to do what living things were supposed to do.

Something was coming. She could feel it.

The sheriff's office sat at the far end of the street, a squat building of warped planks and a roof that sagged in the middle like a saddle that had seen too many hard rides. Hailey pushed open the door, and the familiar creak of the wood and the staleness of the air inside greeted her like an old argument.

Sheriff Hart was where he always was, slouched in his chair, boots propped up on the desk, hat pulled low. The desk was cluttered with papers he never read, warrants he never served, and a coffee cup that had gone cold sometime around the last full moon. He was the kind of man who had mistaken numbness for wisdom, who had watched so many things go wrong that he had decided the safest course was to stop watching altogether.

"Morning, Sheriff," Hailey said, keeping her voice level.

He grunted. Did not move. "What's got you out in this heat?"

"We've got people in town acting strange. More of them today than yesterday."

Sheriff Hart finally lifted his hat, squinting at her the way a man squints at a fly he is deciding whether to swat. "A fever's a fever, Deputy. It'll pass, just like everything else."

"It's not just a fever." Hailey stepped forward, planted her feet. "People are hollow, Sheriff. Like they're not even there."

He rolled his eyes, swung his boots down from the desk. The chair groaned under him. "You're making too much out of nothing, Hailey. People get sick. It's part of life out here. You're a deputy, not a doctor. Leave it to the folks who know better."

The words landed where they always landed, in the soft place between her ribs where all the dismissals had collected over the years, stacking up like stones in a cairn. She had expected this from him. She always expected it. But expectation did not dull the sting. Every day she fought to prove herself, and every day the men around her found new ways to push her back to the starting line.

"You're worked up over nothing," he said, leaning back again, giving her that same condescending look he always did. "Now go on and patrol like you're supposed to. We've got bigger things to worry about than folks feeling a little under the weather."

The door slammed harder than she intended as she left. The frustration burned in her chest like a coal she could not spit out. It was always the same, dismissal, disrespect, as if her concerns were worth less because of who brought them. She took a deep breath of the hot, dust-thickened air. Anger would not fix the epidemic. Anger would not convince the sheriff to act.

But there had to be something she could do.

She caught sight of a group of townsfolk gathered near the saloon, standing in the shade, talking in low voices. Their eyes darted nervously. One of the men, Old Pete, caught Hailey's eye and gave her a slight nod. His face was drawn and pale, the deep lines around his mouth carved deeper than she remembered.

"Everything all right?" Hailey asked, coming to a stop beside them.

Old Pete glanced around, rubbed the back of his neck. "Ain't nothing new, Deputy. Just... folks feeling a little off, you know?"

Hailey frowned, her gaze sweeping the small crowd. A man leaned against the wall, eyes half-closed, body sagging like he could barely hold himself upright. A woman nearby clutched her own arms, skin pale and clammy. But it was not just them. There was an odd stillness to the entire group, a heaviness that clung to the air around them like smoke from a wet fire.

"Pete," Hailey said quietly, stepping closer. "What's really going on?"

He shrugged, but his eyes would not meet hers. "It's that fever, I reckon. Folks been coming down with it for a while now. Makes 'em tired, weak. Some say it's nothing, but..." He trailed off, chewed on his words for a moment, then lowered his voice. "It ain't just the body, Deputy. I've seen Tom Harlan sit on his porch for six hours straight without moving. Six hours. Didn't eat. Didn't drink. Just stared at something none of us could see." He paused again. "Only person who takes this seriously besides you is that Cooper woman. The old one in the boarding house on Tanner Street. She's been burning candles in her windows every night, like she's keeping watch for something. Folks say she knows things. Might be worth a visit, if you're the type."

Hailey's chest tightened. She had felt it too, this sense of something lurking beneath the surface of the town, something more than fever, more than heat exhaustion, more than the slow ordinary death of a place that had outlived its purpose. The signs were everywhere now, even if nobody wanted to name them.

"I'll look into it," she said, more to herself than to Pete. "You stay safe."

"Ain't none of us safe, Deputy." He said it quietly, almost as an afterthought, and turned back to the others.

She moved away from the group, her mind already churning. The town stretched before her, buildings leaning under the weight of too many years, people drifting through the streets in slow, disjointed motions like ghosts who had forgotten they were dead. The more Hailey looked, the more she saw it, the unraveling. The way the town was coming apart at the seams, not with a bang but with a long, low sigh.

And the more she thought about it, the more she realized that the town was not the only thing being haunted by unseen forces.

She turned toward home. The sun beat down harder now, reflecting off the cracked windowpane as she approached the small house she shared with Jake. She took a deep breath before touching the door handle, her hand resting on the worn wood for just a moment longer than usual. Inside was another kind of prison, one that felt even more suffocating than the tense quiet of the streets outside.

## Chapter 1, Part 2: The Other Side of Sleep

Jake sat at the table with his back to the door, but Hailey could read the shape of his anger in the set of his shoulders, in the way his fingers curled around the whiskey glass like he was strangling something small. The bottle on the table was half-empty. The air inside the house was thick with the smell of it, whiskey and stale sweat and something else, something that smelled like resentment left out too long in the heat.

"Where the hell have you been?" His voice cut through the silence, sharp and flat.

"On patrol." Hailey hung her hat by the door. Kept her voice even. Careful. She had learned to modulate every word in this house, to speak the way you walk across rotten floorboards, testing each step before you committed your weight.

Jake stood. The chair scraped against the floor like a blade across bone. "You're always out. What's there to patrol? Ain't no one doing anything in this godforsaken town."

Hailey did not answer. She knew better than to escalate. He had been drinking since noon, probably earlier, and that always meant the air in the house would tighten like a wire until something snapped. The lines of control in her life were blurring, just like the lines between this world and the strange, otherworldly places she found herself in when she slept.

She moved to the kitchen, her footsteps barely audible on the worn floorboards. She could feel his gaze on her back, heavy as a hand.

"I'm trying to keep things together," she said as she opened the pantry door. The shelves were nearly bare, a tin of beans, a half-sack of flour, a jar of preserves with a skin of mold on top. The emptiness inside the pantry mirrored something she did not want to name.

Jake's hand closed around her arm as she passed. His grip was tight. Not the tightest it had been, she had learned to measure his violence in gradations, the way a farmer measures drought, but tight enough. "You ain't doing nothing but pretending you're something you're not." He spat the word like it tasted of ash. "Deputy. You're just playing dress-up."

Hailey pulled free. Kept her face still. Inside, a storm turned slowly, the way storms turn in the high desert, vast and dark and patient. She wanted to scream. She wanted to hit him back. But there was no point. Not tonight. Not in this house where the walls had absorbed so many of these moments that they seemed to lean inward with the weight of them.

"I'm going to bed," she said quietly.

She did not look back as she closed the bedroom door behind her. The click of the latch was the only sound of her defiance. Sleep could not come fast enough. Because when she slept, she left all of this behind. In sleep, she was free.

She lay down on the bed, still dressed, still wearing her boots. The ceiling above her was cracked in a pattern she had memorized months ago, a web of fractures that looked, in certain light, like a map to somewhere she could not reach. She stared at it for a long time, listening to Jake move around in the other room, the clink of glass, the heavy sigh of a man who had lost himself and blamed the whole world for it.

Her thoughts drifted, the way they always did in the space between waking and sleep, to the people she had lost. Her mother, wasting away in a bed that smelled of lavender and sickness. The women in town she could not help. And her brother. Always, eventually, her brother.

She thought of him the way you think of a room you once lived in, the shape of it clear, the details softening with time. He had been older by three years, quieter than her, built from the same stubborn material but with a gentleness she had never managed to replicate. He used to sit on the porch steps in the last light of day, a book open on his knee that he had stopped pretending to read, and say things that sounded like nonsense until years later when they sounded like prophecy. *The dead do not really leave*, he told her once, watching the sun bleed out across the horizon. *They just go somewhere we cannot follow yet.* She had laughed at him then, really laughed, the careless kind of laughing you do when you are young enough to believe that death is a thing that happens to other people. She told him he read too many books, that the dead were dead and the living were the ones who had to figure out the mess they left behind. He had smiled at her the way he always did when she was wrong, not with superiority, but with a kind of patient sadness, as though he could see something coming that she could not. He died eleven months later. And the laugh died with him. She stopped finding humor in the things he had said. She stopped finding humor in most things. Now, lying in this bed in this house in this dying town, with bruises on her arms she hid under long sleeves and a weight on her chest that no doctor could explain, she wondered if he had known. If somewhere in those quiet, bookish bones of his, he had understood that there were doors between this world and whatever came next, and that some people, people like her, would one day be asked to walk through them. She did not laugh anymore. But sometimes, in the space just before sleep, she thought she could hear him. Not his voice. Just the feeling of him. The warmth he left behind in the world, the way a hand leaves warmth on a doorknob after letting go.

The weight of the day dissolved. Her breathing slowed. The familiar sensation of lifting, of being unbound from the heavy fact of her own body, washed over her like warm water rising.

And then she was gone.

The moment her eyes closed, the world shifted. The bruises on her arms dissolved. The suffocating tension of the house, of Jake, of the town and its slow dying, all of it fell away like a coat she had been wearing for so long she had forgotten it was not her skin. She floated, weightless, and the freedom was so absolute it felt like grief in reverse, like the moment before a cry turned inside out into something that might have been joy.

She opened her eyes.

She hovered above an ocean so vast it erased the concept of horizon. The water below was not water as she knew it, it moved with colors that had no names in her language, deep indigos that shifted into molten bronze, surfaces rippling with patterns that seemed to carry meaning, as though the ocean itself were trying to communicate in a tongue older than speech. The light came from everywhere and nowhere, a soft perpetual twilight that turned the sky into a dome of pale violet, and the air tasted of salt and something sweeter, something that reminded her of rain on hot stone.

Hailey dipped lower, her fingers trailing through the surface. The coolness that flowed through her was the opposite of everything she had left behind, the dry heat, the dust, the rough grip of Jake's hand on her arm. Here, her body was not a thing that could be bruised. Here, she was not small.

With a thought, that was all it took, a thought, a wish, a leaning of intention, she dove. Her body sliced the surface without resistance, and she was submerged in a universe that operated on rules she understood instinctively, the way a bird understands wind. She could breathe the water the way she breathed air. She could move in any direction with the slightest inclination of her will. Gravity did not exist here. Fear did not exist here. The only law was freedom.

As she descended, the depths revealed themselves in layers, each one more impossible than the last. Forests of coral that pulsed with bioluminescent blue, their branches swaying in currents she could not feel. Schools of creatures that were part fish and part light, their bodies translucent, their movements synchronized in patterns that looked like music made visible. And then, deeper still, the shadows began to move.

They were enormous. The first one passed beneath her like a mountain drifting through fog, a shape so large that its edges faded before she could comprehend the whole. She descended further, and the shapes resolved. Massive creatures, ancient beyond any reckoning, glided through the deep water with the slow inevitability of continents. One resembled a sea dragon, a hundred feet long at least, its body armored in scales that shifted from deep blue to burnished green with each undulation, its eyes the size of carriage wheels, ancient and knowing and utterly without malice. Another moved like a living cathedral, its body a lattice of bone and cartilage through which light passed and refracted into soft prismatic fans. A third trailed tendrils of phosphorescence behind it, each tendril a hundred yards long, painting the dark water with streaks of cold fire.

Hailey swam among them. She was nothing compared to their size, a mote of dust beside a mountain range, and yet they did not ignore her. The great sea dragon turned one massive eye toward her as she passed, and in that eye she saw something she had never seen directed at her in the waking world, recognition. Not as prey. Not as threat. As kin. These creatures were part of the ancient fabric of this realm, guardians of a dimension that predated language and memory, and they accepted her the way the ocean accepts a river, as something that had always belonged, that had merely been delayed in its arrival.

The thrill of it ran through her like lightning, but it was not the lightning of fear. It was the current of being exactly where she was meant to be. Here, swimming beside creatures old enough to remember the birth of this ocean, she was not a bruised deputy in a dying town. She was not a woman who flinched at the sound of a chair scraping across a floor. She was something vast and capable and unafraid, and the feeling was so intoxicating that she wanted to stay forever, to let the waking world with its dust and its violence fade into a memory she never revisited.

She surfaced and took flight. The transition was seamless, water to air to sky, no boundary between them, just a continuous unfolding of freedom. The wind caught her and carried her higher, and she soared above the ocean with her arms spread wide, the cool air filling her lungs, her hair streaming behind her. Below, the water stretched in every direction, alive with color and motion. Above, the sky was a cathedral of pale violet deepening to indigo at the zenith, dotted with floating islands, impossible masses of stone and earth suspended in the air, their undersides trailing waterfalls that fell upward, their surfaces covered in forests that glowed with an inner phosphorescence, trees whose leaves were made of something between glass and starlight.

For a moment, she let herself forget everything. The bruises. The badge. The dead horse in the street. The hollow-eyed men shuffling out of the saloon. There were no scornful words here, no dismissive glances, no hands that grabbed in the dark. There was only this, endless sky, endless sea, a world of possibilities so vast it made the word "possibility" feel too small. A place where she was untouchable. Untethered.

But then something shifted.

The wind grew colder. Not gradually, it changed in a single breath, the way a room changes when someone steps into the doorway behind you. The light dimmed at the edges of the sky, not like sunset but like something was eating the color from the air. Hailey slowed, hovering, her eyes scanning the horizon.

It was subtle at first. A shadow passing over one of the floating islands, like the silhouette of a cloud except there were no clouds. A chill that settled into her bones despite the impossibility of cold in this place. The sense, unmistakable and primal, of being watched.

She hovered in the air, her breath catching. The stillness that descended over the realm was the opposite of the stillness she had felt in the town, that had been the stillness of exhaustion, of something winding down. This was the stillness of something winding up. A coiled silence. A held breath before the strike.

Her heart quickened. In the distance, at the far edge of where the ocean met the sky, something moved. Not a creature. Not weather. A darkness. It drifted across the horizon like smoke from a fire she could not see, formless and vast, and it carried with it a weight she recognized, the same weight that pressed down on the town, the same heaviness she had felt in the streets, in the air, in the eyes of the people who had stopped seeing.

The presence grew stronger. The cold deepened. Hailey's instinct screamed at her to leave, to pull herself back to the safety of her body before whatever this was took full shape. But she was not built to run. She narrowed her eyes and stared into the distance, trying to focus on the shadow, trying to understand its edges.

She could not make it out. Only the impression of something dark and patient and immeasurably old, moving through the sky the way a predator moves through tall grass, unhurried, certain of the outcome.

A gust of wind slammed into her without warning, spinning her backward through the air. She tumbled, the world wheeling around her, sky and sea trading places in a violent blur. The wind howled in her ears, and for the first time in the astral realm, she felt something close to fear, not the sharp fear of Jake's fist, but a deeper fear, an older one, the fear of something so much larger than herself that her existence barely registered on its awareness.

And then it was over. The wind died. The cold retreated. The shadow pulled back to the horizon and dissolved, or seemed to, leaving only the faintest darkening at the edge of the sky, like a bruise on the face of the world.

Hailey hovered, chest heaving, heart hammering. She looked around. The ocean below was calm again. The sea dragons swam in their ancient patterns, unconcerned. The floating islands glowed. But the memory of the shadow clung to her like the smell of smoke on clothing, invisible, persistent, impossible to shake.

It was gone. For now.

She let herself drift back toward the ocean, the wind carrying her down gently, and she watched the water move beneath her and tried to hold on to the feeling of freedom, of flight, of being more than what the waking world allowed her to be. But the shadow had changed something. The astral realm was no longer just an escape. Something else lived here. Something was watching.

But she could not shake the feeling that the shadow would return. And next time, it might not just pass by.

The realm began to fade, slipping away like a dream that knows it is being remembered. The colors softened, the edges blurred, the vast ocean and the twilight sky dissolving into mist. Hailey felt the familiar pull of her body calling her back, a gravitational tug that started in her chest and spread outward, dragging her down through layers of fading light. The last thing she saw was the glow of the water far below, vast and ancient and indifferent, and the whisper of the wind carrying away the last echo of flight.

When Hailey opened her eyes, reality hit her like a fist.

The ache came first. The bruises on her arms, the sore muscles, the heaviness of limbs that had been weightless moments before. The contrast was savage, like being thrown into a cage after soaring through open sky, like having a door slammed on a view of the infinite. She lay still, staring at the cracked ceiling, her fists clenched so tight her nails bit into her palms.

The house creaked around her, the old wood settling in the wind, the walls close and solid and suffocating. She could hear Jake in the other room, the clink of a bottle, the heavy thud of his boots, each sound a chain link connecting her to a life that felt smaller and meaner every time she returned to it. It felt like she was dragging chains behind her as she sat up, swinging her legs over the side of the bed. The freedom of the astral realm was gone, replaced by the weight of everything she could not escape.

For a brief moment, she considered staying there, lying back down and pretending she was still in the astral realm, floating through a world that did not expect anything of her. But the sun was already high, and the town was waiting. If she did not show up, the sheriff would have yet another reason to brush her off.

With a grimace, she pushed herself up and crossed the room. The mirror on the far wall caught her reflection as she passed, and she paused. Pale. Tired. Dark circles under her eyes that had not been there a few months ago. A bruise on her jawline, faint but visible, from last night's confrontation with Jake. She touched it with her fingertips. The sting was small and familiar.

The anger rose in her chest, but she swallowed it down. What good would it do? Jake was in the other room, already drunk again, and the last thing she needed was another fight before stepping out into the sun to play deputy in a town that barely respected her.

She dressed quickly, pulling on her boots, fastening the worn belt that held her gun. Her fingers brushed the badge on her chest again, and she pressed her thumb against it, hard enough to feel the edges bite into her skin. It was not much. But it was hers. A reminder that even in this decaying town, she had something, not as much power as she wanted, not as much as the men who constantly undermined her, but enough to keep her head held high when she walked through that door.

## Chapter 1, Part 3: The Shadow Advances

Hailey reached for the doorknob and hesitated. Behind this door was Jake, still simmering with whatever resentment had driven him to drink through the night. Beyond Jake was a town unraveling, people falling to an invisible force she could not yet name. And beyond all of that, in some far corner of her mind, was the shadow, the dark presence that had followed her across the astral sky and then retreated, leaving only the memory of cold and the certainty that it would return.

One thing at a time.

She opened the door. Jake sat at the table, slumped, his back to her. He did not turn around as she walked past, though she could feel his attention like heat from a stove she was trying not to touch.

"You going out again?" His voice was low, slurred. "I don't know why you bother."

She did not answer. She gripped the door handle.

"Think you're some kind of sheriff, don't you?" His words followed her through the door, which slammed shut behind her with a crack that echoed down the empty street.

The heat struck her. The sun glared down, and the dusty road stretched out before her, the same as it had been every day, but today it felt heavier. Darker. Something was going to break. She could feel it the way animals feel weather, not in the mind but in the body, in the tightening of the skin, in the quickening of the blood.

She had seen it before. When her mother had gotten sick, years ago. It started the same way, tiredness, confusion, and then the slow, irreversible decay. Her mother had wasted away before Hailey's eyes, and the doctors had not been able to explain it, and Hailey, eight years old and helpless, had watched from a chair beside the bed and learned that there were things in this world that took what they wanted and left nothing. That same helplessness clawed at her now.

But she was not eight years old anymore. She was a deputy with a badge and a gun and a stubbornness that had outlasted every man who had tried to break it.

The sun dipped lower as she walked toward the outskirts of town. The horizon bled into shades of burnt orange and deep purple, the kind of sunset that would have been beautiful if the world beneath it were not falling apart. She passed a group of children near the church, their movements slow and uncoordinated, their play mechanical and joyless. One of them stumbled, catching himself against the side of the building, his face blank, eyes glazed. The others barely reacted. They moved like marionettes whose strings were going slack.

It was getting worse.

Her boots crunched over the dirt road as she made her way toward the boarding house on Tanner Street. She had passed it earlier on patrol, noted the candles in the windows, the smoke, the smell of herbs drifting through the open window. Now she was going inside. If Pete was right about Mrs. Cooper, if the old woman saw what others refused to look at, then Hailey had no excuse not to ask.

The wooden steps creaked under her weight. She knocked. A moment passed, and then the door opened with a slow groan, and Mrs. Cooper appeared in the doorway.

She was smaller than Hailey expected up close. Her face was a map of years, deep lines around the mouth and eyes, skin the color and texture of old leather, hair pulled back in a tight knot that was more gray than black. But her eyes were sharp. Sharper than the sheriff's, sharper than Jake's, sharper than anyone Hailey had met in this town. They were the eyes of a woman who had spent a lifetime seeing things other people refused to look at.

"Deputy Pink," she said in a low, raspy voice, stepping aside. "I wondered when you'd come."

Hailey crossed the threshold, and the world changed. The air inside was cool, dense with the scent of dried flowers and crushed herbs, sage, rosemary, and something earthier, something that smelled like damp soil after rain, like the deep places in the earth where roots go to drink. The room was cluttered with shelves of jars and bundles, books stacked on every surface, candles burning in holders made of dark, pitted metal. It felt like stepping into a different century. The chaos of the town, the heat, the dust, all of it stayed outside, held at the threshold as though it did not dare enter.

"People are getting sick," Hailey said, sitting at the worn table in the center of the room. "And it's not just a fever. Something is happening. Something we haven't seen before."

Mrs. Cooper nodded slowly, moving around the room with steps that were deliberate and unhurried, as though she were navigating by a map only she could see. She did not seem surprised, and that was the most unsettling thing of all.

"I've seen it," the old woman said. "The signs have been there for weeks. People drifting, moving like they've lost a part of themselves. It isn't illness, Deputy. This is something darker."

"Darker how?"

Mrs. Cooper turned, her eyes locking onto Hailey's. "This town has been sitting on something for a long time. Something it doesn't want to reckon with. And now, whatever it is, it's waking up."

Hailey's skin prickled. "There's a presence," she said, and the words came out barely above a whisper, as though speaking them louder might make them more real than she was ready for. "I've felt it. Not just in this world, but... somewhere else. Somewhere I go when I sleep."

Mrs. Cooper's eyes narrowed, but there was no surprise in them. Only confirmation. "You've traveled beyond the veil, haven't you?"

Hailey blinked. She had not expected the old woman to know. She had barely admitted it to herself.

"There are worlds beyond this one," Mrs. Cooper said quietly, settling into the chair across from Hailey. "Places where the rules are different. I've never been there myself, but I've heard the stories, old stories, older than this town, older than the people who built it. People with the gift to move between worlds. People like you. You've seen what's waiting in the shadows, haven't you?"

"I don't know what it is," Hailey said. Her mouth was dry. "But I've felt it. It's watching. And I think it's here, too. In the town. I think it's causing this."

Mrs. Cooper leaned back, her gaze going distant, past the walls of the room, past the town, past the horizon. "If that's true, then it's not just an illness we're dealing with. It's something far more dangerous. Something that's been waiting. Growing stronger."

A cold silence settled between them. Hailey felt the weight of it in her chest, heavy as stone.

"What do I do?" she asked. Her voice trembled, and she hated it.

Mrs. Cooper studied her for a long moment, then stood and moved to one of the shelves that lined the far wall. Her hands moved with certainty among the jars and bottles, bypassing dozens before settling on a small vial near the back, glass, dark, filled with a liquid so thick it barely moved when she tilted it.

"You'll need this when the time comes," she said, placing it in Hailey's hands. "It'll help you keep your mind clear on the other side. But hear me, Deputy." She held Hailey's gaze with an intensity that pinned her to the chair. "This isn't a cure. It's only a tool. The real battle will be fought inside you."

Hailey turned the vial in her fingers. The glass was cool against her skin. The liquid inside was almost black, and it caught the candlelight in a way that made it look alive.

"Thank you," she murmured, slipping it into her coat pocket.

Mrs. Cooper did not respond. But as Hailey stood and moved toward the door, the old woman's voice followed her: "You came here because you already know what's happening, Deputy. You just needed someone to tell you that you aren't crazy."

Hailey paused with her hand on the door frame. She did not turn around. She did not trust her face.

"I needed more than that," she said.

"I know," Mrs. Cooper replied. "But it's a start."

The evening air hit her as she stepped outside, cooler now, the sun barely a sliver on the horizon. The sky was darkening from the west, purple clouds stacking up against the mountains like bruises on the body of the day. Her thoughts were consumed by what she had heard. The sickness was not just a sickness. It had roots in both worlds, hers, and the one she visited in sleep. And if she did not find a way to stop it, it would consume everything.

The once-bustling streets were eerily quiet. The few people who remained outside moved like ghosts, their faces drawn, their eyes unfocused. The sense of dread that had been building all day now seemed to swallow the town whole. Sable Flats felt less like a place she knew and more like a husk, hollowed out by whatever force had taken hold.

She was so deep in thought that she did not see Jake until she was nearly on top of him.

He stood leaning against the wall of the sheriff's office, arms crossed, face twisted into a scowl, the bottle in his hand catching the last of the light. He had followed her. Or he had been waiting. With Jake, the difference hardly mattered.

"What're you doing out here?" His voice was slurred but sharp. "Running around like you're some kinda hero?"

Hailey kept walking. She had no time for this.

"Don't ignore me, Hailey." Louder. Angrier. "You think you're better than me? Running around with that damn badge like it means something?"

She stopped. Turned. Her hands were fists at her sides.

"Leave it alone, Jake."

"Oh, you've got enough, huh?" He pushed off the wall, swaying slightly. "What, you too good for me now?"

She knew where this was going. She had lived through the script so many times she could recite his lines before he spoke them. The escalation. The grab. The morning after, when he would be quiet and small and she would cover the new bruise with her sleeve and pretend the world was not shrinking around her one violent night at a time.

Not tonight.

"I'm warning you, Jake. I don't have time for this."

His hand shot out. Caught her wrist. Pulled her close. His breath was hot and sour with whiskey.

Hailey wrenched free. Stepped back. Her eyes locked on his.

"Don't you ever touch me like that again," she said. Her voice was steady. Her hands were shaking.

For a moment, Jake just stared at her, his face a mask of confusion and drunken rage. Something flickered behind his eyes, not understanding, not remorse, just a momentary blankness, as though he had forgotten who she was and was trying to remember. Then he scoffed, turned away, stumbled toward the saloon.

"Whatever," he muttered, raising the bottle to his lips. "You're just like the rest of 'em. Thinking you're better. You'll see."

She watched him go. The anger in her chest burned white-hot, but beneath it was something colder and more dangerous, the slow, patient fury of a woman who was beginning to understand that she had been afraid of the wrong things.

She stood in the emptying street, breathing hard, trying to steady herself. The fear of Jake, the fear of the epidemic, the fear of the shadow, they braided together in her chest like rope. She pressed her thumb against her badge. The metal was warm from her body heat. Real. Solid.

One thing at a time.

A sound reached her. Distant. Uneven. The shuffle of footsteps that were not quite footsteps, more like the sound of a body being dragged by something that did not care about the body. Hailey's pulse jumped. She scanned the shadows.

A figure stumbled out of the darkness between two buildings. Arms swaying loose. Head hanging. She recognized him, Jonah, a farmer from the outskirts, a man she had spoken to a dozen times at the feed store. A quiet man. Steady.

Nothing about the way he moved was steady now.

His gait was wrong. Too slow, too broken, as if the connection between his brain and his legs had been severed and restrung with something that did not understand how human bodies worked. His head lolled to one side. His eyes were wide and vacant, staring at nothing. His breath came in shallow, wheezing gasps that sounded like wind through a cracked door.

It was as if he was not even there anymore. Just a shell. An empty vessel.

"Jonah." Her voice was firm. Controlled. Her hand went to the revolver at her side. "Jonah, stop."

No response. He kept lurching forward, feet dragging through the dirt.

"Jonah!" Louder now. She stepped toward him, keeping her hand on the gun but not drawing it. This was a neighbor. A man. Not a target.

He stopped. The stillness was worse than the movement. He stood swaying in the night breeze, his body rocking slightly, like a scarecrow that had been cut from its post. The air around him felt colder. Denser. And beneath the sound of the wind, Hailey heard something, a faint hum, low and constant, like the vibration of a wire pulled too tight.

Jonah's head snapped up. His eyes locked onto hers.

The emptiness behind his gaze hit her like a physical blow. It was not lethargy. It was absence. Whatever had made Jonah himself, his thoughts, his humor, the way he used to tip his hat when he passed her on the street, was gone. Hollowed out. And in the empty space behind his eyes, something else looked back at her. Something that did not belong.

His lips parted. A low, guttural sound crawled out of his throat, inhuman and wet. His eyes caught the distant lantern light from the saloon and seemed to glow faintly, not with reflected light but with something generated from whatever now lived inside him.

Without warning, Jonah lunged.

Hailey drew the revolver. Aimed. But she could not pull the trigger. This was Jonah. An innocent man. A neighbor she had known for years. She sidestepped his charge, and his hands clawed at the air where she had been, fingers gnarled and twisted, driven by something that had nothing to do with his will.

"Jonah, stop!" she shouted. It was useless. Whatever controlled him was beyond words, beyond reason, beyond the authority of a badge or a gun.

He came at her again, and she caught his wrist, turned him, used his momentum to push him away. He stumbled, his body jerking like a puppet whose strings were being pulled by a cruel and inexperienced hand.

And then he collapsed. His body hit the dirt and twitched violently, once, twice, three times, before going still.

Hailey stood over him, breath coming in sharp, ragged pulls. She holstered the revolver with hands that shook. She knelt beside him. His chest rose and fell in shallow, uneven breaths. His eyes were open but empty, staring at the sky with the flat gaze of a man who had already left the building of his own body.

She checked his pulse. Weak. Rapid. He was alive, but whatever remained of Jonah was somewhere far from here.

A cold breeze swept through the street. Not the natural cool of evening but something deliberate and focused, as if the temperature had dropped in a column around her and nowhere else. Her breath caught. The hum she had heard grew louder, resolving into something that was almost but not quite a whisper, words just below the threshold of comprehension, spoken in a voice that seemed to come from the ground and the sky at once.

She stood slowly, her eyes sweeping the shadows. She could not see it. But she could feel it. The same presence from the astral realm, the shadow that had darkened the sky, that had spun her through the air like a leaf in a gale. It was here. In the town. In the street where she stood over the body of a man who had been emptied of himself.

The hum swelled. The cold deepened. The shadows between the buildings seemed to breathe, expanding and contracting in rhythm with something she could not see.

Silence.

But the feeling remained, pressing in from all sides. The shadow was closing in. She could not fight it with a gun. She could not fight it with reason. It was something ancient, something that fed on fear and weakness. And right now, it was feeding on the town, turning people like Jonah into hollow shells of themselves.

She took a deep breath. If she was going to stop this thing, she needed to understand it. She needed answers, and there was only one place she could go to get them.

The astral realm.

Her hand went to her coat pocket. Her fingers closed around the cool glass of the vial.

Hailey looked down at Jonah one last time. His breathing had steadied, but his eyes were still vacant, still fixed on something beyond the stars. She could not help him. Not yet. Not from this side.

She turned and walked back to the house. The night had turned cold in earnest now, the wind cutting through her duster, but she kept her eyes forward and her pace steady. There was no room for fear anymore. Fear was a luxury for people who had the option of looking away, and Hailey had lost that option somewhere between the dead horse on Main Street and the emptiness in Jonah's eyes.

The house was dark when she stepped inside. Jake was gone, still at the saloon, drowning whatever he was becoming in whiskey. She locked the door. The silence was enormous.

She moved to the bed and lay down, fully dressed, her coat still on. She pulled the vial from her pocket and held it up. The liquid inside was dark, almost black, and it caught the faint moonlight from the window and swirled with slow, internal currents, as if something inside the glass was alive and waiting.

Mrs. Cooper's words echoed in her mind: *The real battle will be fought inside you.*

Hailey uncorked the vial and brought it to her lips.

The liquid was thick, bitter, and it burned as it slid down her throat, a slow fire that spread from her stomach outward, warming her chest, her arms, her fingertips. She coughed once, the taste clinging to her tongue, and laid back, pulling the blanket over her body. She closed her eyes.

The world began to blur. The edges of the room softened, the hard lines of the ceiling and walls dissolving into something less certain, less fixed. Her breathing slowed. Her body grew heavy, sinking into the mattress, and then lighter, impossibly lighter, as though the heaviness were being peeled away layer by layer, revealing something beneath that was made of nothing but will and intention.

She felt the pull. The departure. The moment when her consciousness separated from her body like a hand lifting from a surface, leaving only the faint impression of warmth.

The astral realm opened before her.

But it was not the same. The sky was darker, the colors muted, the violet haze streaked with veins of ash and charcoal. The ocean below was still vast, still ancient, but its surface was troubled, choppy, as though something beneath was stirring. The air tasted different, colder, sharper, carrying the faint metallic tang of something she could only call dread.

And in the distance, she saw it.

The shadow.

It was clearer now, more defined than it had been during her first flight. It rolled across the horizon like a storm front, but no storm she had ever seen moved with this kind of purpose. Tendrils of darkness reached out from its core, probing the air, stretching toward the floating islands, toward the water, toward her. It moved with the deliberate patience of something that had never been in a hurry because it had never needed to be.

Hailey's heart raced. But her mind was clear, sharper than it had ever been in this place. The vial had burned the fog away, had scoured the uncertainty from her thoughts and left them precise and hard-edged. She could feel the boundary between fear and resolve, and she chose resolve.

She flew toward the shadow.

Because if she did not stop it here, in this realm where it lived, there would be no stopping it in the waking world. And the town, and Jonah, and every hollow-eyed soul drifting through those dust-choked streets, would be lost to the dark.

The wind rose against her. The shadow pulsed. And Hailey Pink, deputy of a dying town, bruised and stubborn and terrified and unbroken, flew into the dark.
