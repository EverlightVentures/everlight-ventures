# Chapter 2: Deeper into the Dream

---

## Chapter 2, Part 1: The Sickness Spreads

Morning came like a wound that would not close.

Hailey stood at the edge of the boardwalk outside the general store, watching the town wake up, or try to. The sun was only half an hour above the ridgeline, the light still thin and copper-colored, but already she could feel the wrongness in it. The air tasted metallic, like the hour before a storm that never arrives. Dust hung in the street without settling, as though the wind itself had forgotten what to do.

She had slept three hours, maybe four. The vial's aftertaste still coated the back of her throat, an acrid bitterness that no amount of water could wash away. But her mind was sharper than it had been in weeks, her senses tuned to a frequency she could not name. Everything felt closer, the creak of the barber's sign swinging on its hinge, the hollow clop of a horse standing unattended outside the bank, the sound of someone coughing behind a closed door two buildings down. She heard all of it. She felt all of it.

Her fingers found the badge on her chest. Cool metal, sun-warmed edges. She pressed her thumb into the star's center point until it stung.

*You are here. You are real. This is the job.*

She stepped off the boardwalk and started her patrol.

The town had never been loud, not in the way cities were supposed to be. It had always been a place of sparse sounds, boots on wood, the distant ring of the blacksmith's hammer, the occasional bark of a dog that had more energy than its owner. But today the silence was different. It was not the silence of early morning or of heat exhaustion. It was the silence of absence.

Hailey walked past the saloon. Its doors were propped open, and inside she could see three men sitting at separate tables, none of them talking. One held a glass of whiskey in both hands, staring into it like a man reading something terrible in the amber. Another sat with his head tilted back, mouth slightly open, eyes half-lidded. The third was simply still, arms flat on the table, face forward, breathing so shallow his chest barely moved.

She had seen tired men before. She had seen drunk men, sick men, men broken by the sun and the work and the slow grinding poverty of frontier life. This was not that. These men looked like they had been emptied from the inside.

She kept walking.

At the corner of Main and the church road, she found Mrs. Delgado sitting on her porch steps with her youngest boy in her lap. The child was maybe four years old. He sat perfectly still, his eyes open but unfocused, his small hands resting palms-up on his knees like a doll someone had arranged and forgotten. Mrs. Delgado rocked back and forth, humming a tune that had no melody, her own gaze distant and glassy.

"Mrs. Delgado," Hailey said. She kept her voice low, steady. "Ma'am, is everything all right?"

The woman looked up slowly. It took her a long time to focus on Hailey's face, as though she were peering through deep water.

"He won't eat," she said. Her voice was flat, stripped of concern, stripped of anything. "Two days now. Just sits."

Hailey knelt down. The boy did not react to her presence. His pupils were dilated wide, black swallowing the brown of his irises. She waved a hand in front of his face. Nothing. Not a flinch, not a blink. He breathed in slow, even intervals, mechanical as a clock.

"How long has he been like this?" Hailey asked.

"Two days," Mrs. Delgado repeated. Then she looked down at the boy as if noticing him for the first time. "Maybe three."

Hailey's stomach tightened. She touched the boy's forehead. He was cool, not feverish, not clammy. Just cool, the way a stone is cool in the shade. She stood up and placed her hand briefly on Mrs. Delgado's shoulder.

"I'm going to send someone to check on you," Hailey said. "Stay here."

Mrs. Delgado nodded but was already looking away, her eyes drifting toward something in the middle distance that Hailey could not see.

---

The sheriff's office smelled like tobacco and stale coffee. Sheriff Hart was behind his desk, hat on, boots up, a newspaper folded on his lap that he clearly was not reading. The window behind him was streaked with grime. A fly circled the room in lazy, purposeless loops.

"Hart," Hailey said, standing in the doorway. "We need to talk about what's happening out there."

He did not look up. "Morning to you too, Deputy."

"There are people in the street who can't keep their eyes open. Mrs. Delgado's boy hasn't eaten in three days. He's four years old and he sits like a stone. Something is wrong."

Hart exhaled through his nose, a sound that was not quite a sigh and not quite a laugh. "People get sick, Hailey. Kids get sick. It's the season. It's the heat."

"It's not the heat."

"Then it's the water. Or the food. Or whatever new hysteria is blowing through town this month." He finally looked at her, and she saw something in his face that stopped her cold. His eyes were glassy. Not in the way a man's eyes get when he is bored or dismissive, but in the way the townspeople's eyes had been looking for days, as if a thin film had been pulled across them, dulling everything behind it.

*He has it too,* Hailey thought, and the realization hit her like a fist to the chest. *Whatever this is. He has it too and he does not know it.*

"You look tired, Sheriff," she said carefully.

"I'm always tired. That's what happens when you get old and your deputy keeps barging in with theories." He waved a hand. "Go patrol. That's what I pay you for."

*You don't pay me enough for any of this,* she thought. But she said nothing. She turned and walked out, the door creaking shut behind her, and stood in the street for a long moment with her hand on her badge, pressing the star into her chest until she could feel the edges bite through her shirt.

The fly inside the office continued its loop. The sheriff did not swat it.

---

She found Old Pete near the livery stable, sitting on an overturned bucket, whittling a piece of wood with slow, deliberate strokes. Pete was one of the few people in town who still seemed fully present, though his hands shook more than they used to, and the lines around his eyes had deepened into trenches.

"Pete," Hailey said, sitting on a hay bale across from him.

He looked up. His eyes were clear, worried, but clear. "Deputy."

"Tell me what you've been seeing."

He set the knife down and rubbed his palms on his knees. "Three more families locked their doors yesterday. The Hendersons' boy hasn't spoken in two days, not a word, not a sound. Just sits at the window and stares out. His mother tried to get him to eat and he knocked the bowl off the table without looking at it." Pete paused, then added quietly: "Like he wasn't even the one who moved his arm."

Hailey leaned forward. "Who else?"

"Tom Bricker. You know Tom, the gambler. He's been at the card table for thirty-six hours straight. Hasn't slept, hasn't eaten. Just plays hand after hand, losing everything. His wife came in crying, tried to drag him out. He looked right through her like she was made of glass." Pete's voice dropped. "And Widow Harlan. She hasn't come out of her house in five days. I brought food to her door, it's still there. All of it."

Pete hesitated, and when he spoke again his voice had dropped to something barely above a whisper. "I looked through her window, Deputy. Just to check she was breathing. She was sitting in that chair by the stove, same as always. But she's, smaller. I don't mean hunched over or curled up. I mean she has less of her. Her wrists are no thicker than kindling. The skin on her hands has gone translucent, I could see the veins underneath, dark as ink lines on tracing paper, could see the tendons sliding when her fingers twitched. And her face..." He trailed off, rubbing his palms on his knees. "Her face is pulling inward, like the skull is rising up through it. Like the grief is eating her from the inside, consuming her actual mass, turning bone into paper and flesh into something that the light passes through. She is not starving, Deputy. She is disappearing. There is physically less of her each day, as if sorrow has a metabolism and it is burning her for fuel."

Hailey's jaw tightened. A gambler who could not stop gambling, his hands breaking themselves to keep dealing. A widow alone who was not merely stopping but dissolving, her body becoming a transparency, a woman-shaped window through which the light of an empty room would eventually pass uninterrupted. Each one pulled deeper into whatever they were already drowning in.

"Do you know about a Dr. Voss?" Hailey asked.

Pete nodded. "Elena Voss. She's the doc out past the tannery. Midwife, mostly, but she's been the closest thing to a real doctor since old Doc Harlow drank himself into the ground. She's been busy, real busy." He looked at Hailey with something that might have been hope. "You should talk to her, Deputy. She's been saying things nobody wants to hear."

---

The clinic was not really a clinic. It was the front room of a weathered clapboard house with a hand-painted sign that read DR. E. VOSS, PHYSICIAN & MIDWIFE. The paint was peeling, but the letters were precise. Hailey knocked twice and the door opened before she could knock a third time.

Dr. Elena Voss was a woman in her early forties with sharp, dark eyes and hands that moved with surgical purpose even when she was just pushing hair from her face. She wore a plain cotton dress under a leather apron stained with iodine and something darker. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbows, and her forearms were lean and strong, the arms of a woman who had spent years pulling babies into a world that did not always want them.

"Deputy Pink," she said. Not a question. She had been expecting this.

"Doctor. Can I come in?"

Voss stepped aside. The front room served as both examination area and office. A long wooden table dominated the center, its surface scrubbed raw but marked with the ghosts of old stains. Shelves lined the walls, filled with jars of herbs, instruments wrapped in cloth, and a row of leather-bound notebooks arranged by year. The room smelled like camphor and clean linen and, underneath it, something sweet and wrong, the faint scent of bodies fighting a war they did not understand.

"Sit," Voss said, pulling a stool toward the table. She did not sit herself. She leaned against the shelf, arms crossed, and studied Hailey with the focused attention of someone who was accustomed to reading symptoms in faces.

"You've been seeing what I've been seeing," Hailey said.

"I've been seeing worse." Voss reached behind her and pulled one of the notebooks from the shelf, flipping it open to a page dense with small, precise handwriting. "I started keeping records two weeks ago, when Margaret Tully came in complaining of exhaustion that sleep wouldn't cure. I thought it was anemia. Iron supplements, rest, fluids. Standard. She got worse. Then I saw Hank Porter, same symptoms, different presentation. His was all in his stomach. Couldn't keep food down, couldn't stop eating. Contradictory. Impossible, by any logic I know." She turned a page. "Then three more. Then eight more. Then I stopped counting individual cases and started looking for the pattern."

"What did you find?"

Voss closed the notebook and looked Hailey directly in the eyes. Her gaze was steady, clinical, but beneath the calm surface there was something Hailey recognized: fear, controlled and compressed but absolutely present.

"This isn't any fever I've ever seen," Voss said. "Their bodies are shutting down from the inside, each one differently. Margaret's blood has thinned so much I can see it, cut her and it runs like water. Hank's digestion has reversed itself. Tom Bricker's hands won't stop moving, I examined him yesterday, and even when he was trying to hold still, his fingers were twitching, reaching for cards that weren't there. Each patient is deteriorating along a different path, but they're all deteriorating at the same rate."

"How is that possible?"

"It's not." Voss set the notebook down with a sharp sound. "That's what I've been trying to tell people. A disease presents consistently. It attacks the lungs, or the liver, or the blood. It does not custom-tailor its destruction to each individual patient. This is something else."

Hailey sat with that for a moment, the weight of it settling in her chest. She thought of Mrs. Delgado's boy sitting like a stone, of Tom Bricker playing cards for thirty-six hours, of Widow Harlan behind her locked door with untouched food rotting on the step.

"Something else," Hailey repeated. "Like what?"

Voss looked at her for a long time. Then she shook her head, not in dismissal, but in genuine uncertainty, the kind that comes from a disciplined mind reaching the edge of what it can explain.

"I don't know," she said. "But whatever it is, it knows them. It knows each of them individually. It finds the crack they already have and pushes. That's all I can tell you."

Hailey stood. Her hand went to her badge. She pressed.

"Thank you, Doctor."

"Elena," Voss said. "If you're the only other person in this town taking this seriously, you can use my first name."

Hailey nodded. "Elena. I'll be back."

She stepped into the street. The sun was higher now, the heat settling in like something solid. Somewhere down the block, a dog barked once and then went quiet, as if it had been told.

Hailey looked up at the sky, pale, bleached, indifferent, and felt the town contracting around her like a fist slowly closing.

## Chapter 2, Part 2: The Forest and the Witch

That night, she drank the last of the water in the basin by her bed and lay down without undressing. Her boots were still on. Her gun belt hung on the bedpost within reach. The badge was still pinned to her chest. She could feel it rising and falling with each breath, a small weight over her heart.

Jake was in the other room. She could hear him, the scrape of his chair, the slow pour of whiskey, the heavy silence that filled the space around him like something with mass. He had not spoken to her since she came home. He had looked at her once, a long, measuring look, and then turned back to his drink. The silence said everything his mouth did not.

*Where were you.*

*Who were you with.*

*You think you're something.*

She closed her eyes and pressed her thumb into the vial in her coat pocket. There was still liquid left, not much, perhaps two more uses. She had already taken the dose tonight, an hour ago, the bitter taste still clinging to the back of her tongue like a word she could not quite speak.

Sleep came faster than it used to. The vial had changed that. Before, she would lie in the dark for an hour or more, listening to Jake's breathing thicken into the uneven rhythm of drunk sleep, her body rigid with the vigilance that came from living with a man whose moods turned like weather. Now, the transition was almost immediate, a loosening, a lifting, as though the floor beneath her opened into sky and she simply fell upward.

---

*She does not fall. She rises.*

*The darkness behind her eyelids dissolves, and what replaces it is not the ancient ocean of before, not the vast gleaming water with its titans moving beneath the surface. This is something else entirely. This is forest.*

*She stands, no, she hovers, at the edge of a woodland so immense it has no horizon. The trees are massive, their trunks wider than buildings, their bark the color of aged iron streaked with veins of deep copper. They rise so high that the canopy is not a canopy at all but a second sky, a vaulted ceiling of interlocking branches so far above her that it fades into a perpetual twilight, neither day nor night but something between, a blue so deep it hums.*

*The air is warm and tastes of rain that has not yet fallen. Every breath fills her with something richer than oxygen, something that feeds parts of her she did not know were hungry. The forest floor is carpeted with moss that glows, not the cold chemical glow of a lantern, but a living warmth, a pulse. Each footfall sends rings of soft amber light rippling outward from her boots, as though the ground itself is responding to her presence.*

*She walks. Or perhaps the forest walks around her, rearranging itself to offer her a path. The trees part gently, their massive roots shifting like the fingers of sleeping giants making room. Between the trunks, she sees things that should not exist in any forest she has ever known, flowers that open and close in slow rhythm, their petals the color of deep ember, each one exhaling a tiny thread of golden vapor that drifts upward and joins the canopy's glow. Vines that move with intention, curling toward her and then retreating, tasting her warmth without quite touching.*

*Hailey reaches out and places her palm flat against the nearest trunk.*

*The bark pulses beneath her hand.*

*It is warm. Alive. Not like wood is alive in the waking world, passively, silently, indifferent to touch. This tree responds. She can feel its pulse like a heartbeat, slow and deep, each beat sending a wave of warmth up through her palm, her wrist, her forearm. The rhythm is ancient, older than language, older than thought. It is the rhythm of something that was here before consciousness existed and will be here after it ends.*

*She pulls her hand away and her fingers glow faintly with the residual warmth, amber light fading slowly from her skin like a memory reluctant to leave.*

*Hailey flies.*

*It is effortless here. She lifts from the moss-bright floor with nothing more than intention, and the forest opens to receive her. She weaves between the trunks, each one a pillar in a cathedral so vast that the word cathedral is too small for it. She spirals upward, her body turning slowly, and the bioluminescent moss below becomes a field of soft fire beneath her, stretching in every direction.*

*Higher. The branches thicken into a lattice of living wood, and she moves through them like water through roots, slipping between gaps that seem made for her. The twilight blue of the canopy sky deepens as she ascends, and she sees, impossibly, beautifully, that the highest branches bear fruit. Not fruit she recognizes. Spheres of soft light, suspended like ornaments, each one containing a tiny, silent explosion of color that repeats endlessly within itself. She reaches for one and it drifts away from her hand, not fleeing but playing, orbiting her fingers before floating higher into the blue.*

*She laughs. The sound is different here, it does not echo so much as spread, absorbed into the forest like rain into earth. The trees seem to lean toward the sound, the way sunflowers turn toward light.*

*For a long, unmeasured stretch of time, she moves through this place with a freedom that makes the waking world feel like a memory of a smaller life. She dives back toward the ground, pulling up at the last moment, her fingertips trailing through the luminous moss and leaving streaks of gold in her wake. She lands on a branch as wide as a road and runs along it, the bark warm and sure beneath her feet, the forest stretching out below her in patterns of light that seem almost deliberate, almost written.*

*And then she feels it.*

*A change in the air. Not a cooling, more like a weight. As though someone has placed a hand on the atmosphere itself and pressed down, gently but firmly. The golden light of the moss dims slightly, not enough to be frightening, but enough to notice. The trees ahead grow denser, their trunks darker, their bark losing its copper veins and turning the color of old iron in shadow. The fruit-spheres in the canopy above go still, their internal explosions of color freezing, suspending, waiting.*

*Hailey slows.*

*Ahead, the forest opens into a clearing. She knows it is a clearing before she sees it, the way one knows the edge of a cliff before reaching it, a change in gravity, in possibility. She descends from the branch and lands softly on the moss, which no longer pulses beneath her feet. It is still warm, but the warmth has gone inward, retreating.*

*The clearing is circular. Perfect. As though something drew it into the forest with intention. The trees at its edge stand like sentinels, their branches intertwining overhead to form a dome of twilight that lets through a single column of blue light, falling from impossibly high above into the center of the space.*

*And in that column of light stands a figure.*

*The witch is old. Not old in the way Mrs. Cooper is old, weathered, human, readable. Old in the way mountains are old. In the way riverbeds are old. She wears a hood of fabric so dark it seems to absorb the light around it, creating a halo of shadow that obscures everything above her mouth. Her hands hang at her sides, long-fingered and still, the color of birch bark in winter. She does not move. She does not breathe. She simply is.*

*The air in the clearing is heavier than the air in the forest. Each step Hailey takes toward the figure feels like walking deeper into water, not resistance exactly, but density. The blue light from above seems to slow as it falls, drifting like dust motes, each particle carrying a faint sound that is not quite music and not quite voice.*

*The witch does not look at Hailey. She does not need to. Her attention fills the clearing like a scent, like temperature, like the pressure before a thunderclap. Hailey can feel herself being known, not watched, not examined, but known, the way a river knows every stone in its bed.*

*Twenty steps. The light thickens. The warmth of the moss is gone entirely, replaced by a coolness that rises from the earth itself. Hailey's hands are tingling, her fingers numb at the tips, and she realizes that the sensation is not cold but gravity, the waking world pulling at her from below, from behind, from some direction that does not exist in this place but is reaching through anyway.*

*Fifteen steps. The witch's mouth is visible now. It is closed, thin-lipped, carved. There is no cruelty in it and no kindness. It is the mouth of something that exists outside those categories entirely.*

*Ten steps. Hailey's body is heavy. Her feet drag against the moss. The twilight blue above has darkened to indigo, and the column of light narrows. She can feel the edges of the clearing pressing inward, the trees leaning closer, the dome contracting.*

*She reaches out her hand.*

*The witch's head tilts, the smallest movement, the first movement, and from beneath the hood, Hailey sees the faintest glint of an eye. Not human. Not animal. Something that contains its own light, the way embers contain fire long after the flame is gone.*

*Hailey's fingertips extend. Inches from the witch's face. The air between them vibrates with a frequency that Hailey feels in her teeth, in her spine, in the space behind her eyes where dreams are built.*

*She is reaching.*

*She is almost --*

---

She woke gasping.

The ceiling was cracked plaster, the same cracks she had memorized in a hundred sleepless hours. The room was dark. The air was stale, thick with the smell of whiskey and unwashed sheets and the faint sour scent of a house that held too much silence.

Jake's arm was across her waist.

It lay there like a chain, heavy and possessive even in sleep. His breathing was deep and ragged, the breath of a man who had drunk himself unconscious. Hailey lay perfectly still, her heart hammering against the badge still pinned to her chest, the metal cold against her skin now, cold and real and small.

She stared at the ceiling and felt the forest fading. The warmth of the moss, the pulse of the bark, the vast cathedral of living wood, all of it dissolving like salt in water, leaving only the taste. The witch's face, that single tilted movement, the eye beneath the hood, these clung to her the way certain dreams cling, not as images but as weight, as something that had been added to her that she could not remove.

Slowly, carefully, she lifted Jake's arm and slid out from beneath it. He grunted but did not wake. She stood in the dark, her boots still on, and pressed her hand against the wall to steady herself.

The vial was changing her. She knew it now with certainty. The astral realm had always been vivid, had always felt more real than the world she returned to, but it had been soft-edged, dream-like, a place she drifted through without purchase. Now it was solid. She could feel things. She could make choices, where to go, what to touch. She had flown between those trees with intention, had placed her hand on that bark deliberately, had walked toward the witch because she wanted to, not because the dream carried her.

More control. More clarity.

But also more awareness of what waited in the deeper places. The witch had not been a threat, or at least, Hailey did not think she was. But the weight in that clearing, the way reality had pulled at her edges the closer she got, the sense of being known by something that existed on a scale she could not comprehend, that was new. That was a door she had not opened before.

She moved to the window and looked out at the street. Empty. Dark. The town slept its fitful, poisoned sleep, and somewhere out there, behind locked doors and drawn curtains, people were being hollowed out from the inside, each one pulled deeper into their own particular darkness.

And she was the only one who could see both sides.

## Chapter 2, Part 3: The Connection

In the morning, Jake was awake before her.

This was unusual. Jake did not wake early by choice, he woke when his body forced him to, when the whiskey wore off and the headache drove him upright. But today he was sitting at the kitchen table when Hailey came out of the bedroom, fully dressed, his hands flat on the wood, his coffee untouched. He did not look at her. He stared at the wall.

"You came home late last night," he said.

It was not a question. It was a statement of fact delivered with the precision of a trap being set. His voice was quiet, which was worse than loud. When Jake yelled, Hailey could brace herself. When he was quiet, the silence filled the house like gas, and she never knew where the spark would come from.

"I was working," she said. She moved to the stove, keeping her back to him, and reached for the kettle. Her hands were steady. She had learned to make her hands steady years ago.

"Working." He said the word the way you say the name of someone who has betrayed you. Slowly, tasting each letter. "Working until midnight. Working when you should be here."

"People are sick, Jake. I told you that."

"People are always sick. That's not your problem."

She poured water into the kettle and set it on the stove. The metal clinked against the iron. She counted to three in her head before turning around.

Jake was looking at her now. His eyes were hard and clear, not glazed, not dulled. Whatever was happening to the town had not touched him yet, and she was not sure whether to feel relief or dread about that. He was himself, fully and terribly himself, and right now that self was a man cataloging every minute she had spent outside this house.

"Where did you go?" he asked.

"I visited the doctor. Dr. Voss, out past the tannery."

"I didn't ask who. I asked where."

"That's the same question, Jake."

He stood. The chair scraped against the floor, and the sound filled the kitchen like a slap. He crossed the room in three strides and stood in front of her, not touching, not yet, but close enough that she could smell the stale whiskey on his breath, could see the veins in his neck standing out like ropes.

"Don't be clever with me, Hailey." His voice was barely above a whisper. "You don't get to be clever with me."

She held his gaze. Her heart was pounding but her face was stone. She had built this mask over years, brick by brick, and she wore it the way she wore the badge, not because it protected her, but because it was the only thing she had.

"I'm going to work," she said.

He stared at her for five seconds, ten, fifteen. The kitchen shrank around them, the walls pressing inward, the ceiling lowering. This was Jake's gift, his terrible, specific gift. He could make a room smaller by standing in it. He could make the air heavier by breathing it. He did not need to raise his fist to control her. He controlled her by being the largest thing in every space they shared.

Then he stepped aside.

Not because he was finished. Because he was saving it. She could see it in his eyes, the way a man marks a debt he intends to collect.

Hailey walked to the door, took her hat from the hook, and stepped outside. The morning sun hit her face like a reprieve, and she exhaled a breath she did not know she had been holding.

---

She walked the town slowly that morning, watching. Watching the way she had been trained to watch, not for criminals or disturbances, but for patterns. The deputy's eye, Mrs. Cooper had once told her, sees what the sheriff's eye misses, because the deputy is still looking.

And Hailey was looking.

She saw Tom Bricker stumble out of the saloon, blinking in the sunlight, his hands trembling at his sides. He had been there all night again. Forty-eight hours now, maybe more. He leaned against the hitching post and stared at his hands as if they belonged to someone else, and then he turned and walked back inside.

But it was his hands that stayed with her. She had seen them up close yesterday, when she tried to pull him from the table. The fingers had begun to curl inward, the joints bending at angles that no living hand should permit, the index and middle fingers of his left hand arched backward past the knuckle, the skin there pulled so taut she could see the white ridges of tendon straining beneath it like cables about to snap. One knuckle had already split, a clean vertical fissure in the skin that wept something clear and pink, and through the gap she had glimpsed the pale wet gleam of bone. Yet he had pulled away from her and gone back to shuffling the deck, the split knuckle leaving smears of watery blood across the face of each card, his ruined fingers performing their task with a precision that the rest of his body had long since abandoned. The sound they made was the worst part, a faint, wet clicking at each joint, like the ticking of a clock built from cartilage and gristle. Tom Bricker was dealing cards with hands that were destroying themselves to deal cards, and he could not stop, and when Hailey had looked into his eyes she had seen not pain but absence, the lights on, the house empty, the hands moving of their own terrible accord.

She saw the Henderson boy standing at his window, exactly as Pete had described, silent, motionless, his small face pressed against the glass. His mother moved behind him, a shadow passing back and forth, her arms full of linens she was not washing, just carrying from room to room.

She saw a woman she did not recognize sitting on the steps of the church, weeping quietly, without sound, her tears falling into the dust and leaving small dark circles that the wind erased almost instantly.

She saw Hank Porter through the open door of the boarding house, and she wished she had not. The man sat at a table heaped with food, bread, jerky, canned peaches with the lids pried open, a wheel of cheese half demolished by his bare hands. He was eating with the frantic, mechanical rhythm of a piston, shoving fistfuls into his mouth without chewing, swallowing in convulsive gulps that made his throat bulge grotesquely with each passage. His stomach had distended so far beyond its natural capacity that it pressed against the edge of the table, the skin visible where his shirt had ridden up, stretched drum-tight and shining, marbled with veins that stood out like dark rivers on a map of ruined country. She could see the shape of what was inside him, the mass of undigested food pressing outward against the abdominal wall in lumps and ridges that shifted when he moved. Even as he ate, his body was rejecting, a thin stream of bile ran from the corner of his mouth, and the smell that drifted through the doorway was the smell of a stomach at war with itself, acid and sweetness and the unmistakable copper of internal bleeding. He looked up as Hailey passed, and his eyes were wet with tears, and his hands did not stop. They could not stop. Whatever had hold of his appetite had disconnected it from pain, from reason, from the body's own screaming insistence that it could hold no more. He was being filled until he burst, and he would burst, and his hands would still be reaching for the next fistful.

Each person, a different symptom. Each person, the same disease.

But it was not a disease. She was becoming more certain of that with every hour. A disease was indiscriminate, it attacked what it attacked and moved on. This was personal. This was specific. This thing, whatever it was, knew them. It knew Tom Bricker's weakness for cards and the way his hands needed to be moving, always moving. It knew Widow Harlan's loneliness, the way she had been slowly disappearing into her own house for years, the way the town had let her. It knew the Henderson boy's silence, the way children go silent when the world becomes too large for their voices.

It found the crack you already had. And it pushed.

The thought followed her into Dr. Voss's office that afternoon.

---

Voss was at her table, her notebooks spread in a fan around her, her sleeves rolled past her elbows. She looked like she had not slept. Her dark eyes were ringed with shadow, but they were sharp, alert, burning with the particular fire of a mind that has found a problem it cannot solve and refuses to stop trying.

"Sit down," she said, not looking up. "I need to show you something."

Hailey sat. Voss turned one of the notebooks toward her and pointed to a column of entries, each one annotated with small, precise symbols that Hailey could not read.

"I've been mapping the patients," Voss said. "Not their symptoms, their histories. Their lives before the sickness." She tapped the page. "Margaret Tully, anxiety, has had it for years, used to take laudanum before I cut her off. Her sickness manifests as blood-thinning, as if her body is trying to become less solid, less present. She's literally fading."

Voss turned to another page. "Hank Porter, compulsive eater, always has been, uses food the way other men use whiskey. His sickness reverses his digestion, he can't stop eating but nothing stays down. His own appetite is destroying him."

Another page. "Tom Bricker, gambler. His hands won't stop. The neural pathways that fire when he plays cards are firing constantly, whether he's at the table or not. His body is performing the addiction even when his mind wants to stop."

Another. "Widow Harlan, isolated herself after her husband died. Refused visitors, refused help, refused connection. Her sickness is total withdrawal. She is shutting down, system by system, as if her body is fulfilling the wish her grief made years ago."

Voss closed the notebook and looked at Hailey. "Do you see it?"

Hailey saw it. She saw it the way you see a shape in clouds, suddenly, all at once, the pattern emerging from the chaos as though it had always been there, waiting to be recognized.

"It's not attacking them," Hailey said slowly. "It's amplifying them."

Voss nodded. "Every patient. Every single one. The sickness finds their deepest compulsion, their addiction, their fear, their wound, and it turns the volume up until it kills them. Margaret's anxiety becomes dissolution. Hank's appetite becomes consumption. Tom's gambling becomes paralysis. The widow's grief becomes absence."

"How?" Hailey breathed.

"I don't know how. I'm a doctor, not a philosopher. But I can tell you what I see: this is not a pathogen. Pathogens don't read people. Pathogens don't know your worst secret and use it against you." Voss leaned forward, and for the first time Hailey saw the controlled fear crack, just slightly, a fracture in the professional armor. "This is something that understands us. Individually. Intimately. And it is using that understanding to take us apart."

Hailey sat in the quiet of the office and felt the two worlds she inhabited pressing against each other like tectonic plates. The astral realm with its forests of living light and figures of ancient power. The waking world with its dust and disease and slow, specific destruction.

They were connected. She had felt it since the first time she projected, that sense that the two places were not separate worlds but different rooms in the same house, divided by a wall so thin that sound passed through it, that pressure from one side bulged the plaster on the other. What she saw in dreams echoed what happened in town. The shadow she had felt watching her in the astral realm was the same force pulling these people apart in the waking world.

*Do subconscious thoughts ripple across dimensions?*

The question surfaced in her mind unbidden, and she held it there, turning it over like a stone. The quantum web, was that what Mrs. Cooper had been describing? A structure beneath reality that connected all things, all thoughts, all wounds? If one person's unresolved pain could bleed through that structure, could it damage everyone it touched? Could trauma, unhealed and enormous, project itself across the invisible scaffolding of existence and find the cracks in other people's walls?

She thought of the witch in the clearing. That ancient, hooded stillness. The eye beneath the hood, containing its own light. The way the clearing had known her, not watched but known, the way a river knows its stones.

The astral realm was not a mirror. It was a source. Or perhaps it was neither, perhaps the relationship between the two worlds was not cause and effect but resonance, two instruments tuned to the same frequency, each one amplifying the other's signal until the noise became unbearable.

*The real battle will be fought inside you.*

Mrs. Cooper's words returned to her, and for the first time, Hailey understood them not as encouragement but as diagnosis.

---

She stayed at Voss's office until the light outside turned amber and then copper and then the particular gray that precedes the desert dark. They talked for hours, Voss about her patients, their patterns, the impossible specificity of each decline; Hailey about what she had seen in the streets, the empty eyes, the puppet-like movements, the silence that had settled over the town like ash from a fire no one could see.

She did not tell Voss about the astral realm. Not yet. That door was not ready to open. But she told her about Mrs. Cooper, about the old woman's certainty that something ancient was at work, and Voss listened with the careful attention of a scientist encountering data that did not fit any known model.

"I don't believe in the supernatural," Voss said finally. "I believe in biology. In chemistry. In the body as a system of systems." She paused, and her fingers tapped the table once. "But my systems don't explain this. And I am not fool enough to dismiss what I cannot explain."

"That makes you the smartest person in this town," Hailey said.

"That makes me the most frightened," Voss corrected. "Smart people know when to be afraid."

Hailey walked home in the dark. The streets were empty, emptier than she had ever seen them. The lamplighter had not made his rounds, and the town sat in near-total darkness, the only light coming from a few windows where lamps still burned, each one a small island of human persistence in a sea of shadow.

She walked slowly, her boots loud on the hard-packed dirt, her hand resting on the butt of her revolver. Not because she expected a gunfight. Because the weight of the gun was real, was solid, was something she could hold onto in a world that felt increasingly like it was dissolving at the edges.

*It finds what you're already broken by. And it amplifies it until it kills you.*

The thought circled in her mind, relentless, wearing a groove into her consciousness the way water wears a groove into stone. She thought of Jake. She thought of her own cracks, the ones she hid beneath the badge, beneath the steady hands, beneath the stone face she wore in his presence. She wondered what the sickness would find if it came for her. She wondered if it already had.

*What are you already broken by, Hailey?*

She did not answer the question. She walked.

Jake was asleep when she got home, slumped in his chair, the bottle empty on the table. She stepped past him, her footsteps soundless, and stood for a moment in the bedroom doorway, looking back at his slumped form. In sleep, the hardness left his face. The anger drained out of his jaw, the controlling vigilance lifted from his brow, and what remained was just a man, tired, damaged, sinking into something he could not name.

She closed the door.

---

Hailey could not sleep.

She lay in the dark, still dressed, badge pressing into her sternum, and stared at the ceiling. The vial was in her pocket but she did not reach for it. Not tonight. Tonight she needed to stay here, in this world, in this body, with its bruises and its fear and its terrible clarity.

She thought about what Voss had said. About the pattern. Each person dying from the thing they were already dying from, just faster. As if something had taken the slow, ordinary decline of human weakness and put it on a track that led to a cliff.

The gambler gambling himself to death. The drinker drinking himself to death. The lonely widow simply stopping. The child sitting like a stone because he had already learned that silence was the safest place.

It was not a disease. It was an amplifier. A force that found the frequency of your deepest wound and vibrated it until you shattered.

She pressed her badge into her chest. The points of the star dug into her skin.

*If it finds what you're broken by...*

She thought of her mother. The slow wasting, the helplessness, the way the doctors had shrugged and turned away. She thought of her brother, the way he used to say the dead do not really leave, they just go somewhere we cannot follow yet. She thought of the bruises on her arms, hidden beneath long sleeves in summer heat. She thought of the mask she wore and the woman who lived behind it and how much distance there was between the two.

*What would it amplify in me?*

The silence. The staying. The part of her that believed, somewhere deep and wordless, that she deserved what Jake gave her. The crack that ran through the center of her life, the one she papered over every morning with duty and determination, the one that opened every night when the lights went out and the house contracted around her.

She closed her eyes. Not to sleep. To think.

The two worlds were not separate. They were the same wound, expressed in different languages. The astral realm spoke in light and moss and ancient figures in clearings. The waking world spoke in dust and disease and the slow collapse of people who had been cracking for years. But the wound was the same. The force behind it was the same.

And if she was going to stop it, she had to understand it. Not fight it, understand it. Understand what it was, where it came from, why it knew them so intimately. Why it could find the exact shape of each person's damage and fit itself inside like a key into a lock.

She opened her eyes.

The room was dark. Jake's breathing in the other room was slow and heavy. The town was silent.

And then she heard it.

Footsteps.

Not inside the house. Outside, in the street. Slow, deliberate, uneven, the sound of boots on hard-packed dirt, moving at a pace that was not quite walking and not quite shuffling. The rhythm was wrong. Too mechanical. Too regular, like a clock that kept imperfect time.

Hailey rose from the bed and moved to the window.

The street was lit only by a thin sliver of moon, the kind of light that creates more shadow than it dispels. She pressed her face to the glass and scanned the road.

She saw him.

A man, she could not tell who from this distance, walking down the center of the street. His arms hung at his sides, swinging slightly with each step, but the swing was wrong, puppet-like, as if his limbs were attached to strings that someone was pulling from above. His head was tilted at an angle that no comfortable posture would produce, cocked to one side, his chin jutting forward. His gait was stiff-legged, each foot rising and falling with the same exact arc, the same exact force, as though his body had forgotten how to walk and was operating from a manual it did not fully understand.

His eyes were open. She could see them even from here, catching the thin moonlight, wide, round, unblinking. Empty. Not sleeping, not dreaming, not drunk. Empty, the way a house is empty after everyone has moved out and the doors have been left open to the weather.

He walked with the focused purposelessness of a machine completing a task it was never designed for. He did not look left or right. He did not adjust his stride for the ruts in the road or the stones in his path. He simply moved forward, in a straight line, aimed at nothing.

Hailey watched, her breath fogging the glass, as the man reached the far end of the street where the feed store's wooden wall blocked his path.

He did not stop.

He walked into the wall. His body struck the wood with a dull, muffled impact, his forehead pressing against the planks. And he did not stop. His legs kept moving, his boots scraping against the dirt, his body pressing forward into the immovable surface as though it were not there, as though he expected at any moment to pass through it and continue walking into whatever lay beyond.

His hands did not rise to catch himself. His head did not turn to find another path. He simply pushed, the muscles of his legs working mechanically, his boots grinding shallow furrows into the earth, his body flattened against the wall of the feed store at three in the morning, pressing forward into solid wood with the blind, empty persistence of something that had forgotten what walls were.

Hailey's hand was on the glass. Her heart hammered against the badge.

She stood at the window for a long time, watching the man push against the wall that would not yield, his legs churning, his eyes open and seeing nothing, his body performing a motion that had no destination and no end.

The moonlight shifted. The shadows moved. The town held its breath.

And somewhere, in a place beneath the surface of everything she knew, Hailey felt the two worlds press against each other like hands on opposite sides of a pane of glass, one made of light and ancient forest, the other made of dust and broken people, and she understood, with a certainty that went deeper than thought, that the wall between them was thinner than anyone knew.

And it was getting thinner still.
