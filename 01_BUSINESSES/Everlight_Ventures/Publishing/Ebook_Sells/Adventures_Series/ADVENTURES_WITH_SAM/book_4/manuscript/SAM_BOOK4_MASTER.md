### SPREAD 1 -- OPENING: Sam in His World
(Full double-page spread. Richly detailed. Reward re-reads.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam stands at the wooden fence at the back of his yard, backpack already on, leaning on the top rail and looking out at the river below. Morning light -- warm gold -- filters through the trees. Robo is beside him, lights blinking a soft, curious yellow. The river is visible through the trees, but something about the way Sam is looking at it tells us he has noticed something is not right. His eyebrows are slightly furrowed. His head is tilted. He is thinking.

Small details to hide in this spread for re-readers: a heron standing motionless at the water's edge in the distance. A plastic bag caught on a branch over the water. A handmade "RIVER WATCH" sign -- blank, unfinished -- leaning against the fence post. A family photo tucked in the outside pocket of Sam's backpack, corner visible.

**Text:**
Sam had a habit of noticing things.

He noticed when his sneakers were on the wrong feet (they were).
He noticed when Robo's lights blinked three times instead of two (something was on his mind).

And this morning, looking out at the river from the back fence,
Sam noticed that something was wrong.

---

### SPREAD 2 -- ORDINARY WORLD: The Riverside Path
(Warm and wide. Establishes the world as beautiful and worth protecting.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam and Robo walking the riverside path on a Saturday morning. The path is shaded, dappled light. Robo's chest display shows a small sun icon -- a good morning signal. The river in the background. Up close it still looks okay. Birds in the trees. A butterfly on a flower. But at the water's edge, closer than Sam has looked before, there are signs -- a few pieces of trash, murky patches near the bank.

**Text:**
Every Saturday, Sam and Robo walked the riverside path.
It was their favorite part of the week --
the big trees, the wide sky,
and the water moving the way water does
when it thinks no one is watching.

"Robo," said Sam. "Does the river look different to you?"

Robo tilted his head. His display showed a small graph.
The line on the graph was going *down.*

"Affirmative," said Robo. "Water quality: declining."

Sam stopped walking.

---

### SPREAD 3 -- THE PROBLEM: The River Is Sick
(Close in. More shadow. The problem lands clearly.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Close-up of the river bank. Trash visible -- a plastic bottle, some tangled netting, a styrofoam cup lodged in the reeds. The water near the bank is murky, greenish. A single heron stands at the water's edge, patient and watchful. She is looking at the water. She has not moved. Robo's display shows a simple downward arrow. Sam's face: serious. Not scared. Serious.

**Text:**
Sam crouched down and looked closely at the bank.

A plastic bottle.
A tangle of old netting.
Something grey and stuck in the reeds.

"Robo," said Sam quietly. "The river is sick."

"The heron has been standing there for a long time,"
said Robo. "She is *waiting.*"

Sam looked at the heron.
The heron did not look back.
She was watching the water.
Patient. Still.

Something in Sam's chest said: *Wait --*

*Why is nobody doing anything?*

---

### SPREAD 4 -- COMMUNITY ELEMENT: Mr. Adeyemi
(Warm, textured. Elder as essential. The past informs the present.)

**Illustration Direction:**
An elderly man -- Mr. Adeyemi -- leaning on the fence at the edge of a community garden that runs alongside the path. He is tall but stooped now, hands rough and kind, wearing a wide-brimmed hat. He is also looking at the river. He holds an old photograph -- the river, clean and bright and full. Sam has stopped to talk to him. Robo is behind Sam, listening. This spread should feel like stepping into history.

**Text:**
A little way up the path stood Mr. Adeyemi,
leaning on his garden fence,
watching the river the same way Sam was.

"I have known this river a long *time,*" he said
when Sam stopped beside him.

He showed Sam an old photograph --
the river, wide and *bright* and *alive.*
Children swimming. A family picnicking on the bank.
The water so clear you could see the bottom.

"It used to *make* a sound like something singing,"
said Mr. Adeyemi. "A *great* sound.
Like the river was glad to be here."

He folded the photograph carefully.

"What happened to it?" Sam asked.

Mr. Adeyemi looked at him.
"The same thing that happens to anything
when people stop paying attention," he said.

### SPREAD 5 -- THE INVITATION: The List
(Purposeful. Sam gets a mission. The adventure begins.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Mr. Adeyemi holds out a folded piece of paper -- a list of names, handwritten. Sam takes it with both hands, looking at it carefully. Robo leans in to look too. His display shows a small arrow pointing outward -- forward motion. The community garden is visible behind Mr. Adeyemi, full of plants and color -- a reminder of what care looks like.

**Text:**
Mr. Adeyemi reached into his pocket
and pulled out a folded piece of paper.

"These are my neighbors," he said.
"People who used to swim in that river as children.
Who used to fish in it, picnic beside it, *name* it their favorite place."

He held it out to Sam.

"Maybe they still care. Maybe they just forgot."

Sam looked at the list.
He looked at Robo.
Robo's display showed a small arrow. Pointing *out.*

"Okay, okay, okay," said Sam. "Let's go."

---

### SPREAD 6 -- THE RESPONSE: Knocking on Doors
(Active, warm, slightly funny. Sam is in motion. His flaw surfaces briefly -- he almost leaves someone out.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam and Robo going door to door through the neighborhood. One neighbor opens their door delighted. Another is skeptical. Ms. Patel is busy (flour on her hands -- she is baking). Old Mr. Rivera uses a cane -- Sam's face goes briefly uncertain before he has an idea. Robo's display shows a clipboard with checkmarks accumulating.

**Text:**
Sam and Robo knocked on every door on the list.

Some neighbors said yes right away, grabbing *gloves* and smiling.
Some said "maybe" -- which Sam decided to count as a yes.

Ms. Patel was busy baking.
She said she would *try* to come by later.

Mr. Rivera said he wanted to help,
but his knees were not what they used to be.
He leaned on his cane.

Sam almost moved on.
Then he stopped.

"Could you be our lookout?" Sam asked.
"Stand on the bridge and wave us over
if you see anyone who wants to join?"

Mr. Rivera smiled like Sam had handed him something.

"Now *that,*" he said, "I can do."

### SPREAD 7 -- THE PHONICS MOMENT: Making the Flyers
(Natural, joyful, visible phonics. Not a lesson -- a moment.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam and Robo at Sam's kitchen table, making flyers. Sam is writing by hand -- big, careful letters. His notebook is open beside him with a word list. Robo's display shows letters with long-vowel patterns highlighted. The flyers are bright and slightly wonky -- a kid made them. Words visible: SAVE OUR LAKE / TIME TO MAKE A CHANGE / COME TO THE RIVERSIDE.

**Text:**
Back home, Sam pulled out paper and markers.

"We need *flyers,*" he said. "So more people *know.*"

He wrote big, careful letters:

*SAVE our lake!*
*TIME to make a change!*
*Come MAKE a difference -- Saturday, nine o'clock.*

"Robo," said Sam, "these words all have a silent e.
Like a secret."

Robo processed this.
"The *e* does not speak," he said.
"But it changes everything around it."

Sam stopped writing.
He thought about that for a second.

"Huh," he said. And then he kept going.

### SPREAD 8 -- THE COMPLICATION: Only Four People Come
(The gut-punch. Sam's impatience surfaces. Robo's best moment.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Saturday morning. The riverside. Sam standing with his gloves on and trash bags, looking at the small group that has arrived: four people. Sam's shoulders are slightly dropped -- disappointment in his posture. The heron is still at the water's edge. Robo is beside Sam, his display showing four small stick figures. The spread should feel wide and a little empty -- the river is big, the group is small.

**Text:**
Saturday morning arrived.

Sam had hoped for twenty people.
He got four.

He stood there with his gloves and his bags
and his too-big backpack,
and he felt his heart sink all the way down
to the bottom of his sneakers.

"It is not enough," he said.

Robo stood beside him.
His display showed four small figures.
Then four more. Then more.

"Four people can still make a start," said Robo.

"It is not *enough,*" said Sam again.

Robo was quiet for a moment.
Then: "Sam. Look at the heron."

Sam looked.

The heron had not moved.
She had been waiting at this river
for much longer than they had.

### SPREAD 9 -- THE DEEPER EMOTIONAL MOMENT: Mr. Adeyemi Sits Beside Sam
(Slow. The emotional heart of the book. The elder says the thing.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam sitting on a rock at the river bank, gloves in his lap, tired. His arms are dirty. The trash pile they have collected is behind him -- large, but the river still looks messy. Mr. Adeyemi walks over slowly with a thermos of water and two cups. He sits beside Sam on a log -- carefully, with his age. He is not lecturing. He is just sitting. The heron is visible across the water.

**Text:**
An hour in, Sam's arms hurt.

He sat down on a rock by the bank.
The river still looked like a mess.
There was so much more to do.

Mr. Adeyemi came over with water
and sat beside him on a log.

For a while, neither of them said anything.

Then Mr. Adeyemi said:
"Do you *know* why I still garden?
Even at my age. Even when my knees say no."

Sam shook his head.

"Because the garden does not *know* if I am tired.
It only *knows* if I showed up."

He handed Sam a cup of water.

"You showed up," said Mr. Adeyemi. "That is the whole thing, son."

Sam drank his water.
He looked at the river.
He thought about his grandfather's tools in the garage --
the ones he had never used, but Grandma kept clean.

He showed up too, Sam thought. Every day. Even when he was tired.

### SPREAD 10 -- THE TRY AGAIN: More People Join
(Energy returns. The plan expands. Community happens naturally.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Sam is back on his feet, notebook out, writing. The original four people are working hard around him. In the background -- coming down the path -- a few more people: a teenager with her little brother, a dad pushing a stroller who has stopped to help. They were not on the list. They just saw people working. Mr. Rivera is visible on the bridge above, watching, waving someone else over.

**Text:**
Sam stood up.

He pulled out his notebook.
He wrote a new plan -- who would collect, who would sort,
who would carry bags to the pile.

And while they worked,
something quiet happened.

A teenager walked by with her little brother.
She stopped. She looked at what they were doing.
She rolled up her sleeves.

A dad with a stroller stopped next.
He parked it on the path, grabbed a bag,
and started working without being asked.

They were not on the list.
They just saw people doing something real
and wanted to be part of it.

From the bridge above,
Mr. Rivera waved someone else over.

This is how it works, Sam thought.
You start. And then you are not alone.

### SPREAD 11 -- THE BREAKTHROUGH: The Heron Walks In
(Quiet triumph. Earned. Not triumphant -- true.)

**Illustration Direction:**
The riverside bank -- cleaned. Not perfect, but clear. The water catching afternoon light. The heron -- who has stood patient all this time -- takes one slow step into the water. Then another. Her long neck bends. She is hunting. She catches a fish. The group has gone still, watching her. Sam is in the center, gloves off now, watching with wide, quiet eyes. Robo's display shows a small heron silhouette. This is the best moment in the book. Give it room.

**Text:**
By afternoon, the bank was clear.

The water was not perfect -- it would *take* more than one day.
But the plastic was gone.
The netting was gone.
The grey thing in the reeds was gone.

And then the heron moved.

One slow step into the water.
Then another.
Her long neck curved down, patient and sure.

She caught a fish.

Nobody said anything.
They all just watched her eat.

Then Sam said --
not triumphant, not loud,
just real and quiet:

"We *made* that possible."

Robo's display showed a small heron.
It glowed.

### SPREAD 12 -- THE RIPPLE: The Community Garden Grows
(Warm and wide. The resolution spreads outward.)

**Illustration Direction:**
The following week. Mr. Adeyemi's community garden, now extended to the edge of the riverside path. Native plants along the border -- wildflowers and native grasses that help filter runoff. Sam is beside Mr. Adeyemi, putting the first plant in the ground, properly dirty. On the fence: a hand-lettered sign in Sam's unmistakable handwriting: RIVER WATCH -- STARTED: TODAY. Below his name, a few other names added in different handwriting. More names coming.

**Text:**
The next week, Mr. Adeyemi extended his garden
all the way to the edge of the riverside path.

Native plants -- ones that helped keep the water clean
even when it rained.

He asked Sam to help put in the first one.

Sam dug with both hands.
The soil smelled like something waking up.

When the plant was in,
Mr. Adeyemi stood up slowly
and looked at what they had done.

"Every garden has to start somewhere," he said.

Sam pulled a marker from his backpack
and wrote on a piece of fence plank:

*RIVER WATCH -- STARTED: TODAY.*

He signed his name.
Mr. Adeyemi signed his.
And then he handed Sam the marker
so there would be room for everyone else.

### SPREAD 13 -- THE GRATITUDE MOMENT: Grandma Is Back
(Slow, golden, connecting generations. The family thread completes.)

**Illustration Direction:**
Evening. Sam and Grandma sitting together on the back steps, looking at the river. The light is the last-of-the-day gold -- warm and almost horizontal. Sam is talking, hands moving, telling the story of the whole day. Grandma is listening -- really listening, the way she does, with her whole body turned toward him. Her hands are in her lap. Behind them through the fence, the river is catching the last light.

**Text:**
That evening, Grandma was back from Aunt Rosa's.

Sam told her everything --
the heron, the list, Mr. Adeyemi,
the four people who became eight,
the plant by the river.

Grandma listened with her whole face.

When he finished, she was quiet for a moment.

Then she said:
"Your grandfather cleaned that *same* stretch of river once.
Before you were born."

Sam looked at her. "How?"

Grandma smiled.
"The *same* way you did.
He just showed up."

Sam sat with that.

The river moved below them,
catching the last of the light.

It still had a long way to go.

But tonight, it looked like it was trying.

### THE LEARNING CORNER
(One page. Age-appropriate. A conversation, not a worksheet.)

**Long Vowels: The Silent E Secret**

In this story, you read lots of words with a silent e at the end.
The e does not say anything -- but it makes the vowel before it say its name!

Look at these pairs:
- tap becomes tape (the a says "ay!")
- bit becomes bite (the i says "eye!")
- hop becomes hope (the o says "oh!")
- cut becomes cute (the u says "you!")

Can you find three long-vowel words in the story?
(Hint: look for words like "make," "time," "home," "name," and "like.")

**Questions to talk about with your grown-up:**
1. What word did Robo explain to Sam about the silent e?
2. Can you think of a word that changes when you add a silent e?
3. What is your favorite long-vowel word from the story?

### THE VALUES MOMENT
(Direct, warm address to the reader.)

Sam learned something important today.

He learned that a superpower is not just something you use to help yourself.
The best superpowers are the ones you use for the world around you --
for the river, for the heron, for the neighbors,
for the people who have not been born yet
who will someday walk down that same path.

Responsibility means taking care of things,
even when no one is making you,
even when it is hard,
even when only four people show up.

Have you ever taken care of something that needed you?
A plant? A friend? A place you love?

What happened when you showed up?

### ACTIVITY SUGGESTION
(Simple, supply-light. Fifteen minutes. No special supplies required.)

**Make a Neighborhood Watch List**

Sam noticed the river needed help.
What do YOU notice in your neighborhood?

Take a walk (with a grown-up) and look for:
- Something beautiful that you want to keep beautiful
- Something that needs a little care
- Something you are grateful for

Write it down (or draw it) in your own notebook.

That list is the beginning of your superpower.
