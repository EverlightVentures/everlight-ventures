## 1. Legal Framework: Social Casinos vs Real Money Gambling {#1-legal-framework}

### The Three Elements of Gambling

Under US law, an activity is generally considered "gambling" when three elements are present simultaneously:

| Element | Definition | How Social Casinos Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| **Consideration** | Money or something of value risked | No real money wagered; virtual currency has no monetary value |
| **Chance** | Outcome determined by luck | Present in casino-style games (this element is unavoidable) |
| **Prize** | Something of value can be won | No real-money prizes; virtual coins cannot be redeemed |

**Key legal principle:** Remove any one element and the activity is generally not classified as gambling. Pure social casinos remove both "consideration" and "prize" -- players spend nothing of real value and win nothing of real value.

### Social Casino Definition

A social casino is a platform where users play casino-style games (blackjack, slots, poker) using virtual currency that:
- Cannot be purchased with real money (or if purchasable, holds no redemption value)
- Cannot be redeemed for cash, prizes, or anything of real-world value
- Exists purely for entertainment

**Legal status:** Pure social casinos are legal in all 50 US states because they do not constitute gambling. They are entertainment software, legally equivalent to a video game.

### Real Money Gambling Definition

Any platform where users wager real money (or currency redeemable for real money) on games of chance. This requires state-level gambling licenses and is currently legal for online casino only in: Connecticut, Delaware, Michigan, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, West Virginia, and Rhode Island.

## 2. The Sweepstakes Model and Its Legal Basis {#2-sweepstakes-model}

### How the Dual-Currency Model Works

The sweepstakes model uses two virtual currencies:

| Currency | How Obtained | Can Be Purchased? | Redeemable for Cash? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gold Coins (GC)** | Purchased or earned free | Yes | No |
| **Sweeps Coins (SC)** | Free bonuses, mail-in requests, daily logins | No (given as bonus with GC purchases) | Yes |

The legal theory: Since Sweeps Coins cannot be directly purchased, and a free method of entry exists (mail-in request or other AMOE), the "consideration" element is absent, making it a promotional sweepstakes rather than gambling.

### Legal Requirements for the Sweepstakes Model (Where Still Legal)

1. **No Purchase Necessary (NPN):** Must offer a genuinely free way to obtain Sweeps Coins
2. **Alternative Method of Entry (AMOE):** Typically a mail-in option where users send a postcard/letter to receive free Sweeps Coins
3. **Equal opportunity:** AMOE entrants must have the same chance to win as purchasers
4. **Official Rules:** Must publish complete sweepstakes rules including eligibility, entry methods, odds disclosure, void-where-prohibited language
5. **Void where prohibited:** Must geoblock users in states where the model is banned

### WARNING: This Model Is Now Illegal in California

AB 831 (effective January 1, 2026) specifically targets this dual-currency model. California defines an "online sweepstakes game" as one that uses "a dual-currency system of payment that allows a person to play or participate in a simulated gambling program for direct or indirect consideration, and for which the person playing may become eligible for a prize or award, cash or cash equivalents."

**Inference (clearly labeled):** Given that Everlight Ventures is based in California and the founder resides there, operating a sweepstakes-model casino from California carries criminal misdemeanor risk even if the platform targets out-of-state users. AB 831 bans "operating" such a game, which could include running the business from California. This needs attorney confirmation.

## 3. Recent California Laws, AG Opinions, and Court Cases (2024-2026) {#3-california-legal-developments}

### AB 831 -- "Gambling: Operation of a Contest or Sweepstakes" (October 2025)

- **Signed:** October 11, 2025 by Governor Newsom
- **Effective:** January 1, 2026
- **Legislative vote:** Unanimous (36-0 Senate, 63-0 Assembly)
- **What it does:** Creates Penal Code Section 337o, making it unlawful to operate an online sweepstakes game using a dual-currency model in California
- **Penalties:** Criminal misdemeanor, up to 1 year in jail, up to $25,000 per violation
- **Vendor liability:** Extends to geolocation providers, payment processors, content suppliers, and media affiliates who "knowingly and willfully" support banned platforms
- **Driving force:** California's 70+ tribal casinos (generating $9B+ annually) lobbied for this; tribal coalitions argued sweepstakes platforms undermined compact-protected gaming exclusivity

### SB 549 -- Tribal Nations Access to Justice Act (September 2024)

- Grants tribal casinos authority to challenge card rooms operating outside state regulations
- Not directly about social casinos but signals strong tribal gaming political power in California

### LA City Attorney vs. Stake.us (August 2025, Ongoing)

- Filed August 28, 2025 by LA City Attorney Hydee Feldstein Soto
- Defendants: Stake.us, Kick (streaming), Evolution Gaming, Hacksaw Gaming
- Alleges Stake.us operated an illegal gambling enterprise in California
- Claims violations of California's Unfair Competition Law and False Advertising Law
- Seeks injunction and recovery of funds lost by California residents
- Stake.us earned approximately $4.7 billion gross revenue in 2024
- **Status:** Ongoing as of March 2026. Evolution and Pragmatic Play reportedly pulled offerings from Stake.us for California users.

### App Store Simulated Casino MDL (September 2025)

- Judge Edward Davila (N.D. Cal.) dismissed all California claims against Apple, Google, and Meta in the "App Store Simulated Casino Litigation"
- This was a class action claiming app stores facilitated gambling through social casino apps
- **Outcome:** Weakened the legal theory that pure social casino games constitute gambling under California law
- **Inference (clearly labeled):** This ruling is favorable for pure social casino operators -- it suggests California courts do not view non-redeemable virtual currency games as gambling

### California AG Opinion on Daily Fantasy Sports

- AG declared DFS illegal in California, characterizing it as sports betting
- While not directly about social casinos, it shows the AG's office is willing to take aggressive positions on gaming gray areas

## 4. Compliance Requirements {#4-compliance-requirements}

### Age Verification

- **Minimum age:** 18+ in most states for social casino (no real money); 21+ in states with regulated gambling
- **Best practice:** Implement age-gate at registration (date of birth entry), with a clear statement that the platform is intended for users 18+
- **Enhanced verification:** For any prize redemption (if applicable), implement full KYC with ID verification

### KYC (Know Your Customer)

For a pure social casino with no cash redemption:
- Basic KYC at registration (email, name, DOB) is sufficient
- No AML/BSA requirements apply since no real money changes hands

For sweepstakes model (where still legal):
- Full KYC required at prize redemption: government-issued ID, proof of address, SSA verification
- AML screening recommended for redemptions above thresholds
- 1099 reporting required for prizes exceeding $600 in a calendar year

### Responsible Gaming Disclosures

Even for free-to-play social casinos, responsible gaming features are considered industry best practice and reduce legal exposure:
- Problem gambling resources displayed prominently (National Council on Problem Gambling hotline: 1-800-522-4700)
- Self-exclusion options
- Session time reminders
- Spending limits on any in-app purchases
- Clear disclaimers that games are for entertainment only

### Data Privacy

- California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA) compliance mandatory for California residents
- Privacy policy must disclose data collection, use, sharing, and deletion rights
- Under-13 protections under COPPA (do not allow users under 13)

## 6. Gambling License vs Sweepstakes Exemptions {#6-license-vs-exemption}

### Gambling License

- Required for any platform where users wager real money on games of chance
- Issued state-by-state (no federal gambling license exists)
- Currently available for online casino in only ~7 states
- Application process: expensive ($100K-$500K+), lengthy (6-18 months), requires background checks on all principals
- Ongoing compliance: regular audits, reporting requirements, responsible gaming mandates

### Sweepstakes Exemption

- No license required because the activity is classified as a promotional sweepstakes, not gambling
- Legal basis: removal of "consideration" element through free entry methods
- Governed by FTC regulations and state sweepstakes/contest laws rather than gaming commissions
- **Risk:** This exemption is under siege. Multiple states banned it in 2025, and class action lawsuits challenge the legal theory.

### Pure Social Casino (No License Needed)

- No gambling license required anywhere
- No sweepstakes registration needed
- Operates as standard entertainment software
- Revenue model: in-app purchases of virtual currency (no redemption), advertising, premium features
- **This is the safest legal path for a California-based founder.**

## 7. Class Action Lawsuits and Outcomes {#7-class-actions}

### Chumba Casino / LuckyLand Slots (VGW Holdings)

- **Kentucky class action:** Settled for $11.75 million. Class included all Kentucky residents who spent $5+ at Chumba/LuckyLand within a 24-hour period between March 2017 and March 2022.
- **Georgia class action (May 2024):** Filed against VGW for operating "just for fun" platforms while facilitating real-money gambling via dual-currency system. Status: ongoing.
- **New Jersey class action:** Filed by Davis & Norris LLP and Brown, LLC, alleging VGW operates illegal online gambling platforms. Status: ongoing.

### Pulsz (Yellow Social Interactive)

- **Settlement 1 (November 2023):** $1.32 million
- **Settlement 2 (April 2024):** $3.6 million
- Both settlements addressed claims that Pulsz operated as an illegal gambling scheme

### Baltimore City Lawsuit (2025)

- Filed by the City of Baltimore against six sweepstakes operators: VGW Holdings, Sweepsteaks Limited (Stake.us), B2Services (McLuck), High 5 Entertainment, Yellow Social Interactive (Pulsz), Blazesoft Ltd. (Fortune Coins)
- Alleges illegal online gambling operations

### Key Legal Theories in These Lawsuits

All lawsuits generally argue:
1. The dual-currency model is a sham -- purchasing Gold Coins that come with "free" Sweeps Coins is functionally equivalent to buying Sweeps Coins
2. The AMOE (mail-in entry) is a fiction -- virtually no one uses it, and the real business model depends on purchases
3. Players are harmed because they lose real money in what is effectively an unregulated casino

### Risk Assessment

**Fact:** Class action exposure is concentrated on platforms using the sweepstakes/dual-currency model with cash redemption. Pure social casino platforms (virtual currency only, no redemption) have significantly lower litigation risk because there is no "lost money" to recover.

**Inference (clearly labeled):** The app store MDL dismissal in California (September 2025) suggests courts may be distinguishing between pure social casino games and sweepstakes casinos, which is favorable for a pure social casino model.

## 8. Recommended Legal Entity Structure {#8-entity-structure}

### For a Bootstrapped Social Casino Startup

**Recommended: California LLC or Delaware LLC**

| Factor | California LLC | Delaware LLC |
|---|---|---|
| **Taxation** | Pass-through (no double taxation) | Pass-through |
| **Formation cost** | ~$70 filing fee + $800/yr franchise tax | ~$90 filing fee, but must register as foreign LLC in CA ($70 + $800/yr) |
| **Legal precedent** | California courts | Delaware Court of Chancery (better for disputes) |
| **Complexity** | Simple | Slightly more complex (two states) |
| **Best for** | Bootstrapping, solo founder | Future investor interest |

### If Planning to Raise Venture Capital

**Recommended: Delaware C-Corporation**

- VCs strongly prefer (often require) Delaware C-Corps
- Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) tax benefits: founders/investors can exclude up to $10M from capital gains
- Established corporate governance framework
- Standard for SAFEs, priced rounds, and eventual exit

### Important Considerations for Gaming Companies

- Some states require specific gaming entity registration even for social casinos if they have in-app purchases
- Consult a gaming attorney about whether California requires any notification or registration for social casino operations
- If incorporating in Delaware but operating from California, you must still register as a foreign entity in California and pay California franchise tax

## 9. Terms of Service Requirements {#9-terms-of-service}

### Essential Provisions for a Social Casino Platform

**1. No Real Money Gambling Disclaimer**
- Explicitly state that the platform does not offer real-money gambling
- Virtual currency has no real-world monetary value
- Virtual currency cannot be exchanged, transferred, or redeemed for real money or anything of value

**2. Eligibility**
- Age requirement (18+ minimum)
- Geographic restrictions (list prohibited jurisdictions)
- Void where prohibited language

**3. Virtual Currency Terms**
- All purchases of virtual currency are final and non-refundable
- Virtual currency is a limited license, not property owned by the user
- Company reserves the right to modify virtual currency balances
- Virtual currency has no cash value and cannot be sold, traded, or transferred

**4. Intellectual Property**
- All game content, designs, and software are proprietary
- Users receive a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable license

**5. Dispute Resolution**
- Mandatory binding arbitration clause (with class action waiver)
- Choice of law (California or Delaware, depending on incorporation)
- Venue selection

**6. Responsible Gaming**
- Links to problem gambling resources
- Self-exclusion procedures
- Company's right to limit or suspend accounts showing problematic behavior

**7. Privacy Policy Integration**
- CCPA/CPRA compliance for California residents
- Data collection, use, and sharing disclosures
- Cookie policy

**8. Indemnification and Limitation of Liability**
- Users indemnify the company against third-party claims
- Cap on company liability (typically limited to amount paid by user in prior 12 months)

**9. Modification Rights**
- Company reserves the right to modify TOS with notice
- Continued use constitutes acceptance of modified terms

**10. Anti-Fraud and Fair Play**
- Prohibition on bots, exploits, and manipulation
- Company's right to void transactions and suspend accounts

## 10. Prize Redemption Legal Considerations {#10-prize-redemption}

### If Operating a Pure Social Casino (Recommended for California)

**No prize redemption = no legal complexity.** Virtual currency stays virtual. Revenue comes from:
- In-app purchases of virtual currency (for entertainment only)
- Ad revenue
- Premium subscriptions (ad-free, cosmetic upgrades, etc.)

### If Operating Sweepstakes Model (NOT Legal in California)

For states where the sweepstakes model remains legal, prize redemption requires:

**Gift Cards:**
- Must comply with state gift card regulations
- Report prizes exceeding $600/year to IRS (1099-MISC)
- Verify recipient identity (KYC) before issuing

**Merchandise:**
- Fair market value must be documented
- Tax reporting for prizes over $600
- Shipping and fulfillment compliance (consumer protection laws)

**Cash/Bank Transfer:**
- Full KYC required (government ID, SSN, proof of address)
- AML screening mandatory
- Must comply with state money transmitter laws -- this is a major compliance burden
- May need Money Services Business (MSB) registration with FinCEN
- State-by-state money transmitter licenses may be required

**Cryptocurrency:**
- IRS treats crypto prizes as taxable income at fair market value
- 1099 reporting required for prizes over $600
- Must comply with state money transmitter laws (most states treat crypto exchange as money transmission)
- Additional compliance with FinCEN regulations
- **Inference (clearly labeled):** Crypto redemption adds significant regulatory complexity and is not recommended for a startup without substantial legal budget
