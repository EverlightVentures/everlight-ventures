## BEFORE READING
*Use these prompts to activate prior knowledge and build anticipation.*

1. **Cover Walk:** Look at the cover together. "What do you think this story is about? What clues does the picture give you?"

2. **Personal Connection:** "Has anything like this ever happened to you or someone you know? What did you do?"

3. **Vocabulary Preview:** Point to 2-3 key vocabulary words (see Vocabulary section). Say each word, show the picture, and ask: "Have you heard this word before? What do you think it means?"

---

## DURING READING
*Pause at natural moments to keep children engaged and check comprehension.*

1. **Mid-story Check-In (pause at a turning point):** "What just happened? How do you think [character] is feeling right now? Show me that face."

2. **Prediction Pause (pause before the solution is revealed):** "Hmm, what do you think [character] is going to do next? What would YOU do?"

3. **Vocabulary in Context (pause at a key vocabulary word):** "The book just said [word]. Look at the picture -- what does that word mean based on what you see?"

---

## AFTER READING
*Give children time to process before jumping into questions.*

1. "What happened at the beginning, middle, and end of the story?"

2. "What was [character's] big problem, and how did they solve it?"

3. "What is one thing [character] did that you would or would not do? Why?"

4. "What lesson do you think this story is trying to teach us?"

5. **Feelings Focus:** "How did [character] feel at the start of the story compared to the end? Have you ever felt that way? What helped you feel better?"

## GRIEF AND LOSS SPECIAL SECTION
*For books that touch on death, loss, or big changes -- only use this section when the book includes those themes.*

**A note for educators:** Young children are not too small to grieve. They need honest, simple language and permission to feel what they feel. The goal is not to "fix" their sadness but to walk alongside them.

Opening statement you can say aloud: "Sometimes we lose someone or something we love very much. That can hurt a lot. And that's okay."

**Gentle Discussion Prompts:**

1. "In the story, [character] is missing [person/pet/thing]. What does [character] do when they feel sad? Is that something you could try?"

2. "When we miss someone, the love we have for them does not go away. What is something you could do to remember that love -- like drawing a picture, telling a story, or looking at a photo?"

3. "It might feel like this sad feeling will never end. But little by little, we learn to carry it. You won't get over it -- but you will get through it. What or who helps you get through hard feelings?"

**Educator Reminder:** If a child discloses a personal loss, acknowledge it privately and follow your school or center's counseling referral protocol.

## EXTENSION ACTIVITIES

**Art:**
Create a "Feelings Weather Map." Give each child a blank circle (the sun). Ask them to draw what their feelings look like today -- sunny, stormy, cloudy with a little rain, or rainbow after rain. Display and share.

**Writing / Dictation:**
"Write or dictate one sentence that tells what you would say to [character] as a friend." For non-writers, use the sentence starter: "I would tell [character] ____________."

**Movement / Dramatization:**
Act it out. Assign children roles from the story and walk through the key events. Pause to ask: "What would you say here?" Encourage children to improvise the dialogue using their own words.

## CIRCLE TIME TIPS
*For reading aloud to a group of 8 or more children.*

1. **Seat children in a tight semicircle and hold the book to the side of your body** so both your face and the illustrations are visible at all times. Children read your face as much as they read the story.

2. **Slow down at emotional moments.** Resist the urge to rush through sad or tense scenes. A 3-second pause after a big moment gives children space to feel it. That processing time is where the learning happens.

3. **Normalize all responses.** If a child laughs at something that seems serious, or cries at something light, respond without judgment: "It sounds like this part is hitting different for each of us -- that's what good books do." Keep the circle safe for every kind of feeling.
