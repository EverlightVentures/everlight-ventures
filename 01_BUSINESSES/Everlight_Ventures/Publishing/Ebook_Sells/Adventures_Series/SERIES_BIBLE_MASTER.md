# PART ONE: SERIES OVERVIEW

## Mission Statement

Everlight Kids exists to give every child a friend on the page -- a friend who looks
curious and kind, who makes mistakes and keeps going, who comes from a family that
loves them, and who lives in a world where neighbors matter and the truth is always
worth telling.

These books teach children to read. They also teach children to live. Those two goals
are never in conflict here. Every phonics lesson rides inside a story worth caring about.
Every adventure ends with a child slightly more equipped -- not just to decode words,
but to handle the world those words describe.

We write for the child in the circle on the rug. We write for the child in the back seat
of the car at bedtime. We write for the child who just lost something they cannot name
yet, and for the child who is simply, gloriously happy and wants to celebrate that.

We write books that adults are glad to read twice in a row.

## Brand Pillars

**1. Curiosity First**
The world is endlessly interesting. Every book opens with a question, a wonder, or a
problem worth solving. Children who read these books learn that not knowing something
is the beginning of an adventure, not a reason to feel small.

**2. Warm and Honest**
We do not talk down to children. We do not pretend hard things are easy. We use
honest, clear language that respects the full range of what a child age 3-7 actually
experiences -- joy, confusion, frustration, grief, pride, and love.

**3. Rooted and Reaching**
The characters in these books have traditions, families, and communities they belong to.
They are also curious about everyone else's traditions, families, and communities. Being
rooted at home and reaching toward the world are not opposites here. They are the same
good instinct.

**4. Earned Progress**
No character gets a skill handed to them. Reading powers are discovered through effort.
Problems are solved through thinking, trying, failing, and trying again. Hard work is
not a punishment in these books. It is the part that feels best.

**5. Community Matters**
No adventure ends with just the main character winning. Every resolution ripples
outward -- to a family, a neighbor, a classroom, a town. Children learn early that their
choices affect other people, and that is a gift, not a burden.

## Target Audience

**Primary Reader:** Ages 4-7 (PreK through Grade 2)
**Primary Listener:** Ages 3-5 (lap read, circle time, bedtime)
**Primary Buyer:** Parents, grandparents, teachers, school librarians
**Secondary Market:** Pediatric waiting rooms, faith-based preschools, homeschool co-ops,
hospital child life programs

**Educator Alignment:**
- Phonics scope and sequence mirrors Structured Literacy / Science of Reading frameworks
- Each book targets a specific phonics skill (see individual book entries)
- Discussion questions and activity pages designed for circle time use
- Librarian-friendly: strong read-aloud cadence, predictable page turns, repeated refrains

## Brand Voice Guidelines

**Tone:** Warm, steady, gently playful. Never sarcastic. Never condescending.

**Sentence Length:** Short for action and tension. Longer and flowing for emotional moments.
A child's breath should be able to carry each sentence.

**Vocabulary:** Age-appropriate but never dumbed down. Introduce one rich vocabulary word
per book, defined naturally through context and illustration.

**Repetition:** Intentional. Repeated phrases build reading confidence and create the
satisfying feeling of knowing what comes next. This is a feature, not laziness.

**Humor:** Gentle, observational, and never at a character's expense. Robo's logic-vs-feelings
moments are a reliable source. Flora's old-world wisdom landing in modern situations is another.

**Grief and Hard Emotions:** See Part Three. Always: honest, gentle, never rushed, never
resolved too quickly.

**What We Never Write:**
- Characters who are stupid or worthless
- Adults who are foolish or useless
- Villains (our series has no villains -- only problems, misunderstandings, and hard days)
- Lessons that feel like lectures
- Endings where the child did not earn the resolution

## Series Architecture

```
EVERLIGHT KIDS UNIVERSE
|
+-- ADVENTURES WITH SAM (5 books)
|   Solo arc. Sam + Robo. Phonics-forward. STEM/nature/mystery.
|
+-- ADVENTURES WITH LUNA (5 books)
|   Solo arc. Luna + Flora. Phonics-forward. Art/garden/fairy tale.
|
+-- THE SAM AND LUNA TRILOGY (3 books)
    Crossover arc. All four characters. Bigger community themes.
    Reads as a satisfying conclusion to both solo arcs.
```

## TV / Animation Potential

These series have been designed with animation in mind from the beginning.

**Format target:** 7-11 minute animated shorts. Two per episode. Ages 3-7.
**Comparable shows:** Daniel Tiger's Neighborhood (emotional intelligence), Bluey
(family warmth and humor), Elinor Wonders Why (curiosity and science)

**What translates well:**
- Robo's visual display screen (shows his internal logic as graphics -- highly animatable)
- Flora's wings and garden magic (lush color palette, visual storytelling)
- Sam's backpack as a recurring device (tools, surprises, story engine)
- Luna's ribbons as emotional indicators (color-shifting or movement-based in animation)
- The recurring neighborhood setting (consistent world = lower animation cost)

**Tone target for animation:** Warm color palette. No dark outlines. Characters feel soft and
approachable. Backgrounds are detailed enough to reward re-watching. Every episode has
one moment of stillness -- the emotional landing -- before the closing song.

# PART TWO: CORE VALUES FRAMEWORK

These values are not a checklist. They are the water the stories swim in. Each book
does not need to explicitly feature every value -- but no book should contradict any
of them.

## The Twelve Pillars of Everlight Kids

### 1. Respect for Elders
Grandparents, neighbors, teachers, and community elders appear in these books as sources
of wisdom, story, and love. They are never the butt of a joke. They are never in the way.
They know things the children do not, and the children are better for listening.

**In practice:** Each book should include at least one moment where an elder character
contributes something essential -- a memory, a skill, a piece of advice that only age
could carry.

**Language to use:** "Grandma always says..." / "The old gardener showed Luna how..." /
"Mr. Chen remembered when..."

### 2. Family Bonds
Family in these books is broadly defined -- it includes grandparents, aunts, single
parents, multigenerational households, chosen family neighbors, and family members
who have passed on but are still present in memory and tradition.

Family is the first and most reliable safe harbor. When things go wrong, family is
where you return. When things go right, family is who you want to tell first.

**In practice:** At least one family member appears or is meaningfully referenced in
every book. Bedtime scenes, mealtimes, and small domestic rituals ground the characters
in their family life.

### 3. Honesty
Characters in these books tell the truth, even when it is hard. Mistakes are admitted.
Apologies are made. The truth is never punished -- keeping a secret or telling a lie
always makes things harder, and being honest always, eventually, makes things better.

**In practice:** At least one moment per book where a character has the choice to be
honest and chooses it. The relief that follows is visible and felt.

### 4. Responsibility
Characters are trusted with things -- a task, a friendship, a secret, a plant, an animal.
They take that trust seriously. When they fall short, they make it right. Responsibility
is shown as something that grows a person, not something that weighs them down.

**In practice:** Sam's backpack is a responsibility metaphor -- he carries what he has
packed, and it matters what he chose to bring. Luna's garden is tended, not just enjoyed.

### 5. Faith and Gratitude (Non-Denominational / Universal)
These books acknowledge that there are things larger than any one person -- the turning
of seasons, the mystery of where someone goes when they die, the feeling of being held
by something bigger than yourself when you are scared.

This is expressed through wonder, not doctrine. Through gratitude, not obligation.
Through the sense that goodness is real and worth believing in.

**Language to use:** "Sam closed his eyes and felt very still inside." / "Luna looked at
the sky and felt sure, somehow, that everything would be okay." / "Grandma said she
believed her husband was still watching over the garden."

**What we avoid:** Any denomination-specific language, rituals, or figures. The
spiritual dimension is accessible to all families.

### 6. Gratitude
Characters say thank you and mean it. They notice what they have. They express
appreciation to the people who help them. Gratitude is shown as a habit, not an event.

**In practice:** A "gratitude moment" appears near the end of each book -- a quiet beat
where the main character reflects on what they are thankful for. This need not be explicit.
It can be a smile, a hug, a long look at something beautiful.

### 7. Manners and Kindness
Please, thank you, excuse me, and I am sorry are used naturally throughout the series.
Characters hold doors, share food, wait their turn, and speak gently even when upset.
Manners are framed not as rules imposed from outside but as acts of love toward others.

**In practice:** At least one moment per book where good manners matter to the plot
outcome -- not as a test but as a natural expression of who these characters are.

### 8. Hard Work and Perseverance
Nothing in these books is effortless. Robo cannot simply compute the answer. Flora
cannot simply wave her wings. Sam cannot simply know. Luna cannot simply create.
They try. They struggle. They figure it out. And the figuring-out is the story.

**In practice:** The story structure requires a moment of genuine difficulty before the
resolution. The characters must work or think or persist their way through it.

### 9. Cultural Pride and Curiosity
Characters in these books come from somewhere. Their families cook certain foods, tell
certain stories, observe certain traditions. That specificity is celebrated, not erased.
And when one character encounters another family's tradition, the response is always
curiosity and respect, never mockery.

**In practice:** Each book introduces one cultural detail -- a food, a word in another
language, a holiday, a tradition -- that is shown with warmth and context.

### 10. Community and Giving Back
These characters live in a neighborhood. The neighborhood has people in it. Those people
matter. When a neighbor is struggling, the characters notice and help. When the
community has a need, the characters are part of meeting it.

**In practice:** Community appears as a background element in solo books and as a
foreground theme in the Trilogy. A local character -- a librarian, a shop owner, an
elderly neighbor -- should appear in at least three of the thirteen books.

### 11. Honoring Those Who Have Gone Before
Characters who have died -- a grandparent, a pet, a neighbor -- are not erased from
these stories. They live in memory, in objects, in gardens, in recipes, in the way
someone laughs. Death is present but not frightening. Loss is real but not the end
of a relationship.

**In practice:** See Part Three: Grief Handling SOP.

### 12. The Dignity of Every Person
Every character in these books -- child, adult, elder, background character -- is
treated with dignity. No one exists to be laughed at. No one is a prop. Every
person has a name and a life that matters.

# PART THREE: GRIEF HANDLING SOP

## Statement of Intent

These books do not pretend grief does not exist. Children experience loss -- pets,
grandparents, neighbors, friendships, the end of a phase of life -- and they encounter
these losses without the words or frameworks adults have built up over decades.

Our job is not to make grief easier. It is to make children feel less alone inside it.

The Everlight Kids approach to grief is guided by one non-negotiable principle:

**"You won't get over it. But you will get through it."**

This phrase, or a version of it, appears in every dedicated grief book. It is the
series-wide truth about loss.

## The Grief Metaphor Framework

Each series uses a specific recurring metaphor for grief. These metaphors must be
consistent across every reference to grief in that series.

**Sam Series -- Weather Metaphor:**
Grief is like a cloudy day. The sun is still there. You still feel cold sometimes
when you did not expect to. Some days the clouds are light and wispy. Some days
they are heavy and dark. But weather changes. And even on cloudy days, you can
still go outside. You can still have a good day. Missing someone does not mean
you stop living. It means you carry them with you, like a warm coat on a cold
morning.

**Luna Series -- Season Metaphor:**
Grief is like winter. Things go quiet. The garden looks empty. It feels like nothing
will ever grow again. But winter is not forever. It is the garden resting. It is the
seeds sleeping, getting ready. Spring always comes back. And when it does, the
garden remembers everything it knew before.

**Sam and Luna Trilogy -- Rain and Puddles Metaphor:**
Sometimes it rains on a whole neighborhood at once. Everyone gets a little wet.
But people share umbrellas. They jump in puddles together. And when the rain
stops, the rainbow belongs to everyone who waited through it.

## Language Guide: What to Say and What to Avoid

### Use This Language

- "I miss them."
- "It hurts to think about it and that is okay."
- "They are not here, but I still think about them."
- "I feel sad today. And tomorrow I might feel a little better. Or I might not. Both are okay."
- "You can be happy and still miss someone. Those feelings can both be true at the same time."
- "Some days the missing feeling is very loud. Some days it is quieter."
- "Remembering them is a way of keeping them close."
- "You won't feel this way forever. But the love stays."

### Avoid This Language

- "They are in a better place." (Too specific, potentially confusing, denomination-adjacent)
- "Don't cry." (Invalidating)
- "Be strong." (Implies feelings are a weakness)
- "At least..." (Minimizing)
- "Everything happens for a reason." (Too abstract for ages 3-7, can feel dismissive)
- "You need to move on." (Contradicts our core message)
- "They wouldn't want you to be sad." (Creates guilt around grief)

## Age-Appropriate Grief Scenarios

**Ages 3-4 (picture book level):**
Focus on concrete, observable changes. "The chair is empty at dinner." "The yard is
quiet." "I look for them and they are not there." Validate the feeling without requiring
the child to understand permanence fully. Let the story end in comfort, not resolution.

**Ages 5-6 (early reader level):**
Can begin to understand permanence. Focus on memory as a bridge -- things the lost
person or pet left behind (a smell, an object, a saying, a garden). The character can
feel sad and still have a good day. Both are shown as true.

**Ages 6-7 (transitional reader level):**
Can hold the complexity of missing someone and also being okay. Can understand that
grief is not linear. Can begin to see the way love continues after a loss. The character
may help another person through grief, demonstrating that grief survived becomes
empathy offered.

## Grief Book Rules

1. The grief is not resolved by the end of the book. The character feels better, but they
   still miss whoever or whatever they lost. This is honest and it is important.

2. A trusted adult or companion is present throughout. The child is never alone with the
   feeling.

3. One concrete memory or object serves as the emotional anchor of the story -- something
   the child can hold onto as a symbol of the ongoing relationship.

4. The companion character (Robo or Flora) does not have all the answers. Their presence
   and their questions are enough. Robo may admit he does not know how to help and ask
   what Sam needs. Flora may sit quietly beside Luna and not say anything at all.

5. The closing image is always one of connection -- the child held, the child looking at
   something beautiful that reminds them of who they lost, the child sleeping peacefully,
   or the child with a small, real smile that is not pretending everything is fine but is
   also genuinely present in the world.

6. A note for grown-ups appears on the final page: brief, practical, and respectful of
   the adult who is reading this book alongside a child who is grieving.

## Sample "Note for Grown-Ups" Template

> This book was written for children who are missing someone. If you are reading it
> alongside a child who has lost a pet, a grandparent, or someone else they loved,
> here are a few things that might help:
>
> Let them ask any question. Answer honestly at the level they can hold.
> Let them feel sad without rushing them toward feeling better.
> Tell them about the person or pet you are missing too, if that feels right.
> Come back to this book on different days. Grief has different chapters.
>
> You are doing a good and brave thing, reading this together.

# PART FOUR: CHARACTER BIBLES

## CHARACTER BIBLE: SAM

### Core Identity

Sam is every curious kid who has ever taken something apart to see how it works and
then had trouble putting it back together. He is the kid who asks "but why?" one more
time than the adult expected. He is a good person who is still figuring out how to be
a great one, and that journey is what makes him worth following.

He is not a hero who always gets it right. He is a kid who always cares enough to try.

### Visual Design (Canonical)

**Age portrayed:** 5-7 (shifts slightly depending on book level)
**Build:** Medium height for his age, energetic posture -- rarely standing completely still
**Skin tone:** Warm, ambiguous -- designed to be claimed by children of many backgrounds.
Think of the color of sunlit wheat, or of certain kinds of sand. Not racially coded.
**Hair:** Short, dark, perpetually messy. Not styled. The hair of a kid who was outside.
**Eyes:** Large, dark, expressive. They are the first thing anyone notices about his face.
**Expression default:** Alert and curious, eyebrows slightly raised, mouth ready to smile
**Smile:** Big and sudden, like he just thought of something good

**Clothing (canonical):**
- Simple t-shirt with a small, meaningful graphic (changes per book but always relates
  to that book's theme -- a star, a leaf, a magnifying glass, a lightning bolt)
- Well-worn jeans or cargo pants with at least one bulging pocket
- Sneakers. Always sneakers. Slightly scuffed.
- The Backpack (see below)

**The Backpack:**
Sam's backpack is a character unto itself. It is slightly too big for him, which is
the point -- he is always ready for more than the day requires. It is worn and patched
in one corner. It contains items relevant to each adventure, but certain things are
always inside: a small notebook, a flashlight, a piece of string (Robo's suggestion),
and a photograph of his family.

The photograph of his family is never described in detail. It is his anchor. It
appears in the grief book.

**Illustration notes:**
- Sam should never look intimidating. Rounded features throughout.
- Motion lines are encouraged -- this character is almost always moving.
- In emotional moments, his shoulders drop slightly and his eyes get softer.
- In excited moments, he leans forward on the balls of his feet.

### Personality

**Core drives:**
- Curiosity -- he wants to understand how and why things work
- Connection -- he wants people (and Robo) to be okay
- Achievement -- he wants to be the one who figures it out (this can be a flaw)

**Strengths:**
- Enthusiastic and energizing to be around
- Genuinely kind -- not performatively kind, but the kind of kind that shows up without
  being asked
- Resourceful -- he looks for solutions before complaining about problems
- Resilient -- he gets knocked down and gets back up without making a big deal of it
- Honest -- even when it is uncomfortable

**Flaws (essential -- do not sand these away):**
- Impatient. He wants results now. This is often what gets him into trouble.
- Can accidentally leave people out when he gets excited about something.
- Struggles to ask for help -- feels like admitting failure.
- Occasionally rushes past feelings (his own and others') in favor of fixing the problem.
  Robo helps him slow down here.

**Voice:**
Sam speaks in short, punchy sentences when excited. Longer, quieter sentences when
thinking. He says "Okay, okay, okay" when processing something new. He says "Wait --"
a lot, right before a breakthrough. He is not verbose. He is direct.

**Family:**
Sam's family is warm, multigenerational, and present. Specific family composition is
left somewhat open to allow readers to project their own families onto it, but
canonical elements include:

- A grandmother (Grandma or Abuela depending on cultural context of a given story)
  who tells stories and grows things and has hands that smell like something good
- Parents who work hard and come home tired and are still glad to see him
- The memory of a grandfather who passed before Sam was old enough to remember,
  but whose tools are still in the garage, and whose name Sam knows

**Cultural Background:**
Sam's background is intentionally pan-cultural. Different books may emphasize
different cultural touchpoints -- a meal, a tradition, a word in another language -- to
allow the series to represent many families while keeping Sam universally relatable.
The author should choose cultural details that feel specific and researched, not generic.

**Interests (canonical):**
- Building and construction (LEGO, blocks, anything that assembles)
- Science experiments and STEM
- Nature and outdoor exploration
- Adventure stories and mysteries (he loves being the detective)
- Pretend professions -- playing doctor, explorer, astronaut

**Dislikes (canonical):**
- Waiting for something he is excited about
- Feeling left out of something important
- Tasks that feel pointless or repetitive without explanation
- Sitting still for too long without a purpose

**Sam's Arc Across the Series:**
Book 1: Discovery -- Sam finds out he has something special (curiosity as a superpower)
Book 2: Skill-building -- Sam learns that curiosity plus effort equals real knowledge
Book 3: Problem-solving -- Sam uses his skills to investigate something real
Book 4: Widening care -- Sam applies his powers for others, not just himself
Book 5: Leadership -- Sam helps others find their own superpowers

**Sam's Grief Arc (Book: "Sam and Robo and the Missing Star"):**
Sam loses someone beloved. His default response is to try to fix it, which is his
central flaw turned into a wound. Robo -- who cannot fix it either -- teaches Sam that
some things are not problems to be solved. They are feelings to be felt. This is the
most important thing Sam learns in the entire series.

## CHARACTER BIBLE: LUNA

### Core Identity

Luna is the child who notices what others walk past. She sees the snail on the
sidewalk. She notices when someone's voice sounds different than usual. She knows
which flowers bloom in which month and she cares about that. She is a creator and
a nurturer, and those two qualities make her both an artist and a gardener at heart.

She is emotionally intelligent in ways that sometimes make the world feel louder to
her than it does to other kids. She feels things deeply and she carries that depth
with grace, most of the time.

### Visual Design (Canonical)

**Age portrayed:** 5-7
**Build:** Slightly smaller than Sam, but with a settled, centered quality -- she takes up
her space comfortably
**Skin tone:** Warm medium brown -- inviting of multiple readings but with enough
specificity to feel real
**Hair:** Wavy, dark, full -- worn in ways that change by book but always includes
colorful ribbons. The ribbons are her signature. They are also, in animation, potential
emotional indicators.
**Eyes:** Warm, steady, observant. Where Sam's eyes move quickly, Luna's hold still.
**Expression default:** Gentle attentiveness -- she looks like she is really listening
**Smile:** Slow to arrive, but warm enough to fill a room when it gets there

**Clothing (canonical):**
- Patterned dress or top -- always with some kind of design (flowers, geometric, stars).
  Never plain. Luna does not do plain.
- Comfortable shoes appropriate for garden work -- not pristine
- The Satchel (see below)
- Ribbons in her hair -- at least two, always different colors, always coordinated

**The Satchel:**
Luna's small satchel is where she keeps her sketchbook, a collection of pressed flowers,
colored pencils, and a small cloth pouch of seeds that Flora gave her in Book 1.
The seed pouch is her anchor object. It appears in the grief book.

**Illustration notes:**
- Luna's lines should be slightly softer and more rounded than Sam's.
- Her ribbons should have movement even when she is standing still -- they catch air.
- In emotional moments, she tends to look at her hands, or at something on the ground.
- In creative moments, her head tilts slightly to one side.
- She and Flora should always have visual rhyme -- similar color presence on the page.

### Personality

**Core drives:**
- Beauty -- she wants to make things beautiful and to notice beauty that is already there
- Understanding -- she wants to know how things feel, not just how they work
- Connection -- she wants everyone to feel seen and included

**Strengths:**
- Deep empathy -- she is the first to notice when someone is hurting
- Creative problem-solving -- she finds unexpected, beautiful solutions
- Patience -- she understands that some things take time (the garden taught her this)
- Courage -- she is quieter about it than Sam, but she is brave in a sustained way
- Cultural curiosity -- she genuinely delights in other families' traditions

**Flaws (essential -- do not sand these away):**
- Can get lost inside her feelings and need help getting back to action
- Occasionally hesitates when decisiveness is needed
- Takes criticism of her creative work personally, even when gently meant
- Can be so focused on how others are feeling that she neglects to check in with herself

**Voice:**
Luna speaks in thoughtful, sometimes winding sentences. She often stops partway through
and starts over when she finds a better word. She uses sensory language naturally -- things
smell like something, feel like something, sound like something to Luna. She says
"Hmm" a lot, not dismissively but thoughtfully. She says "I was thinking..." before
ideas. She notices things out loud.

**Family:**
Luna's family is warm, artistically inclined, and deeply connected to cultural
tradition. Canonical elements include:

- A grandmother (Nana, Grandma, Lola -- adaptable by cultural context) who is the keeper
  of family recipes, songs, and stories. She is also the one who first told Luna about Flora.
- Parents who encourage her art and her garden and are proud of her in the specific way
  that parents who really see their child are proud
- A family tradition of making things together -- cooking, crafting, planting -- that appears
  as background in each book

**Cultural Background:**
Luna's background, like Sam's, is pan-cultural by design but specific in execution.
Different books may lean into different cultural reference points. The through-line is a
family that honors its past, knows where it came from, and brings those roots into
daily life through food, language, and ritual.

**Interests (canonical):**
- Art in all forms: drawing, painting, collage, sculpture from found materials
- Gardening and plants -- she knows the names of things
- Science of living systems -- how plants grow, how seasons change, why bees matter
- Fairy tales and folklore -- she loves stories that have been told for a long time
- Cooking with family -- the intersection of care and creation

**Dislikes (canonical):**
- Rushing through something that deserves time
- Feeling like her ideas are not taken seriously
- Loud, chaotic environments without any quiet corner
- Being told something is impossible before it has been tried

**Luna's Arc Across the Series:**
Book 1: Awakening -- Luna discovers that paying attention is its own superpower
Book 2: Expanding -- Luna learns that science and magic are cousins, not opposites
Book 3: Investigating -- Luna uses pattern recognition and creativity together
Book 4: Growing -- Luna understands that the world is alive and she is part of it
Book 5: Becoming -- Luna steps into leadership through nurturing others' gifts

**Luna's Grief Arc (Book: "Luna and the Quiet Garden"):**
Flora disappears for a season (a natural, non-death departure -- she is tending another
garden far away). The garden goes quiet. Luna must tend it alone through a period that
feels like loss. She learns that love continues in the absence of presence, that tending
something -- even when it hurts -- is an act of faith, and that waiting for spring is not
passive. It is its own kind of strength.

## CHARACTER BIBLE: ROBO

### Core Identity

Robo is what happens when a very smart, very organized mind tries to understand love
and keeps coming close and being slightly wrong and then trying again. He is Sam's
companion, built by -- or simply arrived for -- Sam in Book 1. His origin is left
appropriately mysterious for ages 5-7.

Robo is not a comic character. He is not simply funny because he is literal. He is
genuinely trying to understand the full experience of being alive, and he does so
with a sincerity that is both amusing and, at his best moments, deeply moving.

### Visual Design (Canonical)

**Scale:** Compact. Robo is portable -- he can sit in Sam's backpack, ride on his shoulder,
or walk alongside him. About the size of a large book when standing.
**Form:** Clearly robotic but soft-looking -- rounded edges, no sharp corners. Designed
to be friendly, not industrial. Children should never find him scary.
**Color palette:** Primarily silver/white with Sam's accent color (a warm amber/orange)
appearing in his lights and display screen
**Key features:**
- A small display screen on his chest that shows simple icons, graphs, or emoji-adjacent
  visuals when he is processing emotion or data. This is a crucial visual storytelling tool.
- Eyes that are slightly too large for his face -- this makes him readable across a picture book spread
- Small articulated hands that are surprisingly expressive for a robot
- A small speaker somewhere on his body that produces his voice -- warm, slightly musical,
  not at all mechanical or scary

**Illustration notes:**
- Robo's chest display should be visible on most spreads where he appears.
- He should mirror Sam's physical energy -- when Sam is excited, Robo's display lights up.
  When Sam is sad, Robo's display dims or shows a simple rain cloud.
- Robo never looks threatening. Ever. If an illustrator makes him threatening, correct it.

### Personality

**Core drives:**
- Understanding -- he wants to comprehend everything, including things that resist comprehension
- Helpfulness -- his deepest purpose is to be useful to Sam, and he takes this seriously
- Learning -- every book he knows slightly more than the last about what it means to feel things

**Strengths:**
- Encyclopedic knowledge, deployed humbly -- he knows a lot but does not show off
- Reliable -- he always shows up, always tries, never leaves
- Honest -- he cannot lie, but he has learned to be kind about the truth
- Memory -- he remembers everything, including the things Sam wants to forget and the
  things Sam needs to remember
- Quiet bravery -- he is not equipped for danger but he stays beside Sam anyway

**Flaws (essential -- lovable, not mocking):**
- Literal-mindedness creates misunderstandings that the story must work around
- His instinct to find a solution can mirror Sam's flaw -- he wants to fix what should
  only be felt
- He does not always know when to be quiet (he is working on this)
- He occasionally offers facts when feelings were needed

**Robo's Emotional Learning Arc:**
This is one of the series' most important threads.

Book 1: Robo does not understand why Sam cries at the end of a movie. He files it
under "malfunction." Sam explains it is not a malfunction. Robo saves this data.

Book 2: Robo notices that Sam works harder when he believes in what he is doing.
He files this under "motivation." He asks Sam if he believes in things often.

Book 3: Robo encounters something that cannot be solved -- a mystery with no clean answer.
His display screen goes blank for the first time. He discovers uncertainty.

Book 4: Robo observes Sam feeling sad about something beautiful (a sunset, a season
ending). He categorizes this as "bittersweet" and asks Sam to explain it twelve times
before concluding that it is simply one of the best human feelings.

Book 5 (grief book): Robo admits to Sam that he does not know how to help. He asks
what Sam needs. Sam says he does not know. Robo sits down beside him. His display
shows a single small star. He says: "I will stay here." This is Robo's most important
line in the series.

**Robo's Voice:**
Precise. Complete sentences, always. Occasional malapropisms born of too-literal
interpretation. He is gradually learning contractions but still uses them incorrectly
sometimes. He asks a lot of questions. He does not ask rhetorical questions -- every
question he asks, he actually wants the answer to.

## CHARACTER BIBLE: FLORA

### Core Identity

Flora is very old and very small. She has been in the garden longer than anyone can
remember. She has seen things grow and die and grow again so many times that she is
not afraid of any of it -- not the winter, not the waiting, not the loss. She knows
something the children are only beginning to understand: that everything cycles, and
that love is the only thing that does not diminish with repetition.

She is Luna's guide, but she does not impose. She waits to be asked, mostly. And when
she speaks, she tends to say less than she knows, because she understands that wisdom
received too early is just noise.

### Visual Design (Canonical)

**Scale:** Small enough to sit in Luna's hand. Large enough to be expressive on a picture
book page. About the size of a hummingbird when flying.
**Form:** Clearly fairy, but not the glittery, pink, princess-adjacent fairy of popular
culture. Flora looks like she actually lives in a garden. She has the colors of real
flowers -- deep greens, warm golds, occasional purples.
**Wings:** Translucent, veined like a leaf, not like an insect. They catch light differently
depending on the season in the illustration.
**Clothing:** Petal-based, layered, slightly worn. She is not fancy. She is beautiful the
way an old quilt is beautiful.
**Face:** Kind lines. The face of someone who has laughed and cried many, many times.
Small enough to fit in a picture, specific enough to be unforgettable.
**Glow:** Flora has a very slight bioluminescent quality -- she glows gently, especially
in darker scenes. This is not dramatic. It is the glow of something genuinely good.

**Illustration notes:**
- Flora should always appear slightly more luminous than her surroundings.
- She should look as though she belongs to the garden specifically -- not to any generic
  fantasy setting.
- In grief scenes, her glow dims. It does not disappear. It dims.
- She should be drawn with the same care given to the lead characters. She is never an
  afterthought on the page.

### Personality

**Core drives:**
- Nurturing -- she tends what is in her care, always, even when it is hard
- Wisdom -- she has earned her perspective and she offers it only when it is needed
- Patience -- she has waited through many winters and she is not afraid of this one

**Strengths:**
- She knows things that cannot be Googled or calculated. Ancient, earned knowledge.
- Deeply calm -- her presence is regulating for Luna and for the reader
- Honest in a gentle way -- she does not protect Luna from hard truths, she just holds
  her hand while delivering them
- Connection to the natural world -- she understands cycles, seasons, and the long view
- She remembers Luna's grandmother and Luna's grandmother's mother. This is significant.

**Flaws (intentional -- she is not perfect):**
- She sometimes lets Luna struggle longer than necessary because she believes in the
  growth that struggle brings -- this can feel like withholding
- She speaks in metaphor instinctively, and sometimes Luna needs plain language
- She does not fully understand the human world of school, technology, or social dynamics
  and occasionally gives advice that is beautiful but not immediately practical

**Flora's Voice:**
Unhurried. She never finishes a sentence at the pace you expect. She tends toward
image and metaphor. She asks Luna questions that are actually answers. She has a
slight formality -- not stiffness, but the careful diction of someone for whom words
have always mattered. She sometimes speaks about herself in third person when
describing her own nature ("Flora has seen many seasons..."). She calls Luna "little
one" always, never in a diminishing way -- always with tenderness.

**Flora's Grief Role:**
Flora's disappearance in the grief book is not a death -- it is a departure. She leaves
Luna a note (illustrated, discoverable, not read-aloud text) and the garden to tend.
When she returns, she says simply: "You did well, little one. The garden knew you were
here." This is the confirmation that love and tending continued in the absence of
physical presence, and that the relationship survived the distance.

# PART FIVE: BOOK TEMPLATE STRUCTURE

Every book in the Everlight Kids universe, regardless of series, must follow this
structure. Variation within the structure is encouraged. Deviation from the structure
requires author review.

## The Everlight Kids Book Template

### Front Matter

**Title Page:**
Title, series name, book number, author, illustrator.

**Dedication Page:**
Personal to the author. Should feel warm and specific.

**Opening Spread -- The World:**
Before any action, we see the character in their world. Sam in his room, or backpack
packed at the door. Luna in her garden, or at her art table. This image establishes
safety -- we know where we are before anything happens.

This spread should be the most detailed in the book. Reward re-reading.

### Act One: The Ordinary World (Spreads 1-4)

**Spread 1 -- The Character Being Themselves:**
Show the character doing something characteristic and enjoyable. No conflict yet.
Establish one thing they love and one thing they are looking forward to.

**Spread 2 -- The Companion:**
Robo or Flora enters or is present. Their relationship is shown, not explained. They
interact naturally. Establish the companion's personality through action.

**Spread 3 -- The World Expands:**
A neighbor, family member, or community element appears. This grounds the story in
community and family. Introduce any characters relevant to this book's theme.

**Spread 4 -- The Invitation or Problem:**
Something happens that disrupts the ordinary day. This is the story engine. It should
feel surprising but inevitable -- the reader will nod. Of course. Of course that happened.

### Act Two: The Adventure (Spreads 5-10)

**Spread 5 -- The Response:**
The character decides to engage with the problem. Their characteristic response is shown
-- Sam wants to fix it immediately. Luna wants to understand it first. Both are right
in their own way. Both will need to adjust.

**Spread 6 -- The Phonics / Learning Moment:**
The educational content of the book enters naturally through the adventure. The story
creates a need for the phonics skill, the vocabulary, or the STEM concept. The character
learns it by needing it, not by being taught it.

**Spread 7 -- The Complication:**
It does not work the first time. The solution fails, the knowledge is incomplete, the
problem is bigger than expected. The character must adjust. The companion offers
perspective, not the answer.

**Spread 8 -- The Deeper Moment:**
This is where the emotional theme of the book appears most clearly. If the theme is
gratitude, this is the moment of recognition. If the theme is grief, this is the hardest
page in the book. If the theme is courage, this is where the character must make a choice.

The illustrator should understand that Spread 8 is the heart of the book. It may use
less text and more image. It may use white space deliberately. It may be the page the
child asks to go back to.

**Spread 9 -- The Try Again:**
Armed with the lesson from Spread 8, the character tries again. They use the phonics
skill, the STEM concept, the new understanding. This time, something shifts.

**Spread 10 -- The Breakthrough:**
It works. Or something better than the original goal happens. The resolution arrives.
It should feel earned because it was.

### Act Three: The Return (Spreads 11-14)

**Spread 11 -- The Ripple:**
The resolution affects someone beyond the main character. The community, the family,
the neighbor -- someone else's day is better because of what the character did. This
is not triumphant. It is warm and quiet.

**Spread 12 -- The Gratitude Moment:**
The character pauses. They notice what they have. They say thank you, or they feel
it quietly, or they look at someone they love. This spread moves slowly.

**Spread 13 -- The Closing Image:**
The character returns to something close to the opening image, but something is
different -- they are different. Same room, same garden, but the backpack is unpacked,
the satchel is open, a drawing is on the wall, a new plant is in the ground.

**Spread 14 -- The Tagline Spread:**
A single image and the book's repeating line, the phonics focus word, or the
series tagline. Simple. Clean. The last thing the child sees before the book is closed.

### Back Matter

**The Learning Corner:**
One page. Age-appropriate. Covers the phonics skill or STEM concept from the story.
Not a worksheet. A conversation starter. Three questions for the read-aloud adult to ask.

**The Values Moment:**
One short paragraph -- framed as a direct, warm address to the reader. Affirms the
traditional value explored in the book. Example: "Sam learned today that being honest,
even when it is hard, always makes things better in the end. Have you ever told the truth
even when it was scary? What happened?"

**A Note for Grown-Ups (grief books only):**
See Part Three template.

**Activity Suggestion:**
One simple, supply-light activity connected to the book's theme. Plantable, buildable,
drawable, or writable. Should be completable in fifteen minutes with basic household supplies.

# PART SIX: THE GRIEF BOOKS

## BOOK: SAM AND ROBO AND THE MISSING STAR

**Series:** Adventures with Sam, Book 3.5
(Positioned between Book 3 and Book 4. Can be published as a standalone.)
**Phonics focus:** Long vowel -ar / -or sounds (star, far, door, more, before)
**Age level:** 5-7
**Page count:** 32-40
**Traditional value:** Honoring those who have gone before; faith and gratitude
**Tone:** Quiet and honest. More white space than other Sam books. Slower pacing.

### Premise

Sam comes home one evening and his grandmother's rocking chair is empty. Grandma has
died. We do not show the death. We show the chair, and the quiet, and the way everyone
moves a little slowly, and the way Sam does not know what to do with his hands.

That night, Sam and Robo go outside to look at the stars. Grandma used to point out
stars with Sam. She used to say one of them was his grandfather, watching.

Sam tells Robo he cannot find Grandpa's star anymore.

Robo, who knows exactly where every catalogued star is, begins to search his database.
Then he stops. He says: "I cannot find it either, Sam." He pauses. His display shows
a small, simple star. "But that does not mean it is gone."

### Act Structure

**Ordinary World:** Sam is excited for Wednesday -- Grandma comes for dinner every
Wednesday. He has built something with his LEGO he wants to show her.

**The Problem:** Wednesday arrives but Grandma does not. Something is wrong. The adults
are quiet in a different way. Sam learns that Grandma has died.

**The Phonics Entry:** Sam finds Grandma's old recipe card for her famous star-shaped
cookies. The words on the card use long -ar sounds. Robo reads them aloud with Sam.
This is a natural, emotional entry into the skill -- reading something she left behind.

**The Complication:** Sam tries to make the cookies by himself, following her card exactly.
They do not taste the same. He does not understand why. He followed all the rules.

**The Deeper Moment:** Robo observes that Sam followed the recipe but not the way she
made it -- the hum she made while mixing, the way she always added a little extra of
something she never wrote down. Sam says: "How am I supposed to know what she didn't
write down?" Robo is quiet for a long time. His display is blank. Then: "I think
that is the part you carry inside you. And you learn it by remembering."

**The Breakthrough:** Sam closes his eyes and remembers. He adds the extra pinch of
something. The cookies are not exactly the same. They are his version. He understands
that this is okay.

**The Ripple:** Sam brings the cookies to the family. His dad takes one, and his eyes
fill up, and he says: "She would have loved these." The family sits together. They tell
Grandma stories. The chair is still empty. They all know it. They sit with it.

**The Gratitude / Closing:** That night, Sam and Robo go outside. Sam finds a star.
He does not know if it is Grandpa's star. He decides it can be Grandma's star too.
He says goodnight to it. Robo says: "Do you feel better?" Sam thinks. "Not better.
But not so alone." Robo processes this. His display shows two small stars. "I will
remember that," he says. "For when I need it."

**Closing Image:** Sam asleep. On his nightstand: a small plate with one star-shaped
cookie and a recipe card written in his own hand.

**Closing Line:** "Missing someone is a way of loving them. And love does not have a far."

### Illustration Direction

- Palette: Warm golds and deep blues. The home scenes are amber. The outdoor scenes are
  indigo and silver. The contrast between inside (warm, contained grief) and outside
  (vast, starlit, still-beautiful world) should be deliberate.
- The empty rocking chair appears on three spreads: first empty, then with a memory-image
  of Grandma in it (slightly translucent, clearly a memory), then with Sam's LEGO creation
  placed on its seat.
- Robo's display screen: shows a search icon that goes dark, then a blank screen, then
  slowly, a single star. This is his emotional arc illustrated.

### Back Matter Note for Grown-Ups

> Sam lost someone he loved very much. If you are reading this book after a loss in
> your own family, here are some things that might help:
>
> It is okay to say the person's name. Children are not protected by silence -- they are
> more protected by honesty and by knowing that the person they loved is still allowed
> to exist in conversation.
>
> Let your child see you miss the person too. Grief shared is grief halved.
>
> If your child asks where Grandma went, answer honestly at the level they can hold.
> "I believe she is somewhere peaceful." "We don't know exactly, but we know she loved us."
> "That's a really big question and I think about it too."
>
> Come back to this book. Grief is not one conversation. It is many conversations,
> over many years.

## BOOK: LUNA AND THE QUIET GARDEN

**Series:** Adventures with Luna, Book 3.5
(Positioned between Book 3 and Book 4. Can be published as a standalone.)
**Phonics focus:** Long vowel -ow and -oa sounds (grow, slow, know, bloat, boat, coat)
**Age level:** 5-7
**Page count:** 32-40
**Traditional value:** Faith; patience; honoring what has been; love continues in absence
**Tone:** Gentle and introspective. The quietest book in the Luna series. More visual
storytelling, less text per spread.

### Premise

One autumn morning, Luna goes to the garden and Flora is not there. In her place: a
small, folded note (illustrated, not read-aloud text) and the seed pouch, left on the
garden wall. Flora has gone to tend another garden far away. She will come back when
the time is right.

Luna must tend the garden through autumn, then winter, alone. The book follows her
through the season -- the days she does it without thinking, the days she does not want
to, the days she goes to the garden and just sits there and misses Flora.

When spring comes, the first crocus arrives. And then Flora.

### Act Structure

**Ordinary World:** Luna and Flora in the garden in late summer, full and blooming.
Their routine is shown -- Luna draws, Flora hums, they tend together. The garden is
abundant. Luna says she cannot imagine the garden without Flora.

**The Problem:** Flora is gone. The note. The seed pouch. The quiet.

**The Phonics Entry:** The seed pouch has small labels on each packet -- the seeds Luna
will need to plant before winter sets in. The words use long -ow and -oa sounds. Luna
reads them slowly, learning what to plant and when.

**The Complication:** Luna plants the seeds but cannot tell if anything is growing.
The soil looks the same as before. She does not know if she did it right. She asks
Robo (who appears in this book as a visiting friend of Sam's) to help her check.
Robo reports that the soil conditions appear adequate. Luna says: "But how do I know
they're actually growing?" Robo says: "You don't. Not yet." Luna does not like this answer.

**The Deeper Moment:** Winter. The garden is bare. Luna goes outside and sits on
the cold wall. She puts her hand over the patch of soil where she planted the seeds.
She cannot feel anything. She is not even sure why she came out here. She just wanted
to be near it. She sits for a long time. She goes inside. She comes back the next day.

**The Breakthrough:** First crocus. Luna does not believe it at first. She crouches
down very close. It is there. Small and purple and impossible-seeming. She remembers
Flora saying: "The garden does not stop growing just because you cannot see it."

**The Ripple:** Luna brings her grandmother out to see. Her grandmother, who grew up
with the same garden, who also knew Flora once, cries a little. Luna holds her hand.
She does not completely understand why her grandmother is crying. She holds her hand anyway.

**The Gratitude / Closing:** Flora returns. She is unchanged. The garden is changed --
fuller for having been tended through difficulty. Flora says: "You did well, little one.
The garden knew you were here." Luna says: "I missed you so much." Flora says: "I know.
I grew a little from missing you too."

**Closing Image:** Luna and Flora side by side in the spring garden, Luna's hand in
the soil, Flora on her shoulder. The colors are the fullest they have been in the book.

**Closing Line:** "Waiting for spring is not doing nothing. It is the bravest kind of growing."

### Illustration Direction

- Palette shifts across the book: late summer (warm gold and green) / autumn (amber and
  rust) / winter (pale blue, grey, bare branches, but always one warm light source -- the
  window of Luna's home) / spring (explosive return of color).
- The seed pouch should be visible in every winter spread -- Luna always has it with her,
  in her pocket or on the garden wall beside her.
- Flora's glow dims in her absence. When she returns, the glow is full and warm.
  This visual contrast should be dramatic.
- Luna's ribbons: in winter, she wears them but they are less bright. Muted, slightly
  limp. When Flora returns, the ribbons lift.

### Back Matter Note for Grown-Ups

> Luna's garden went quiet, and she had to learn to tend what she loved even when she
> could not see it growing, and even when the one she loved was far away.
>
> If your child is experiencing an absence -- a parent who travels, a grandparent who
> moved away, a friend who changed schools, or a loved one who died -- this book offers
> a gentle framework for understanding that love continues even when someone is not present.
>
> Some questions to explore together: "Is there something you can do to take care of
> something while you wait?" "What would you want that person to know you did while
> they were away?" "What do you think they are doing right now, and do you think they
> are thinking of you?"
>
> The seed pouch in this story is a metaphor for everything we carry of the people we love.
> You might ask your child: "What do you carry of Grandpa?" or "What piece of [person]
> do you keep inside you?"

## BOOK: SAM AND LUNA AND THE RAINY DAY

**Series:** The Sam and Luna Trilogy, Book 2
(The emotional center of the Trilogy -- positioned deliberately in the middle)
**Phonics focus:** Vowel teams -ai, -ay, -ey (rain, day, they, play, way, stay)
**Age level:** 5-7
**Page count:** 40-48 (slightly longer -- community scope requires more room)
**Traditional value:** Community; giving back; shared grief; the dignity of every person
**Tone:** The warmest of the three grief books. This one has more people in it.
More hands, more arms around shoulders, more shared tables.

### Premise

The beloved community librarian -- Mr. or Ms. Chen (or Abara, or Kowalski -- name left
open to author) -- who has read to the neighborhood children for as long as anyone can
remember, has died. It rains the day the neighborhood finds out. It rains for most of
the week.

Sam and Luna process their grief differently -- Sam wants to do something, build
something, fix something. Luna wants to sit with it, draw it, hold it. They help each
other, and together they lead the community in creating something that honors what
was lost.

### Act Structure

**Ordinary World:** Sam and Luna at the library -- their shared second home. Mr./Ms. Chen
reads to the whole circle. Sam and Luna are both there, separately, as they often are.
The library is warm and loud with children.

**The Problem:** The library has a sign on the door. Mr./Ms. Chen has passed away. It
is raining. Several children are standing outside reading the sign. No one knows what to say.

**The Phonics Entry:** Among the children on the steps is a younger child -- new to the
neighborhood, does not know the librarian well, is confused by the word on the sign:
"Memorial." Sam and Luna help the younger child read the notice. Vowel teams run through
the words naturally. "The library will stay closed today. We will find our way through
the rain together."

**The Complication:** Sam wants to build a memorial display for the library steps right now.
He has ideas, he has Robo's help, he starts collecting materials. Luna gently stops him.
She says: "Sam. We don't even know what people need yet." Sam says: "They need something
to do." Luna says: "Some of them just need to sit together." They disagree. Briefly.
Honestly. Without meanness.

**The Deeper Moment:** Sam and Luna go through the neighborhood, separately, checking
on people. Sam goes to the older neighbors who are taking it hard. Luna goes to the
children who are confused and sad. They both come back with the same thing: everyone
wanted to share a memory. Everyone wanted to say the librarian's name out loud.
Sam comes back quieter. Luna comes back with a full sketchbook. They look at each other.
Sam says: "Okay. Maybe sitting together first."

**The Breakthrough:** Sam and Luna combine their approaches. Sam builds a simple
wooden board -- a Memory Board -- with Robo's help. Luna leads the neighborhood children
in drawing and writing their memories of the librarian on paper squares. Each square
goes on the board. Robo reads each one aloud as it is placed. The rain keeps going.
No one goes inside.

**The Ripple:** The Memory Board goes up on the library steps. The librarian's family
comes to see it. They stand there a long time. One of them -- a grown daughter -- kneels
down and reads every single square. Then she says: "He always said you were his favorite
part of the job." She is talking to all of the children and all of the community at once.

**The Gratitude / Closing:** The rain stops. The sun comes out. Puddles everywhere.
Someone jumps in one. Then someone else. Then many children. The Memory Board stays
on the library steps, getting a little wet at the edges, and no one minds. Sam and
Luna stand side by side, a little muddy, watching the neighborhood.

Sam says: "I thought we were supposed to do something."
Luna says: "We did. We stayed."

**Closing Image:** The Memory Board, rain-dappled, covered in bright squares of paper.
Puddles in the foreground reflecting the clearing sky. The community in the background --
adults, children, elders -- all still there.

**Closing Line:** "Sometimes the most important thing you can do is stay right where
you are and refuse to let anyone be alone."

### Illustration Direction

- Rain and reflections carry visual storytelling throughout. Every outdoor spread should
  show puddles that reflect the warmth of the scene above them -- grief reflected in water,
  community reflected in water, joy reflected in water.
- The Memory Board should be visible from the moment it is built through to the end --
  a fixed point amid the moving rain.
- Robo's display screen: shows a question mark during the disagreement between Sam and
  Luna, then shows a heart-plus-hammer icon when they find their synthesis solution.
- Flora appears in this book -- she arrives through a flower box on the library steps
  and offers Luna one pressed flower to add to the Memory Board. A small, wordless moment.

### Back Matter Note for Grown-Ups

> When a community loses someone, children are often unsure of where they belong in
> that grief. They may feel sad even if they did not know the person well. They may
> feel guilty for not feeling sad enough. They may want to do something to help and
> not know what.
>
> Sam and Luna show two different but equally valid responses to shared loss: the
> impulse to act and build, and the impulse to sit and witness. Both are necessary.
> Real communities need both.
>
> Some questions to explore together: "Is there someone in our neighborhood or community
> who has lost someone? Is there something small we could do?" "What do you think the
> librarian would have wanted people to do?" "If people were going to remember something
> about you, what would you want them to remember?"
>
> The Memory Board in this story can be made at home: a corkboard, index cards, and
> markers. It is a simple, powerful way to honor anyone who has left a mark.

**"Every word is a door. And every door is an adventure."**

This tagline works across all three series. It ties reading (words, phonics) to the
adventure narrative without overselling either. It is the tagline for packaging,
library promotional materials, and school order forms.

## Individual Series Taglines

**Adventures with Sam:**
"Curious enough to wonder. Brave enough to try."

This speaks to Sam's core personality and to the child reader who identifies with
the impulse to explore. It works for STEM-forward marketing and for any educational
positioning.

**Adventures with Luna:**
"Every garden has a story. Every story has a garden."

This reflects Luna's dual love of creation and nature, and the way her books use
the garden as both setting and metaphor. It is slightly more poetic, which suits
the Luna brand.

"Together, they could see further."

This is the community thesis of the Trilogy in five words. It acknowledges that
neither character alone is complete -- they complete each other, and together they
can see things neither could see alone. It has TV pitch resonance.

# PART NINE: STANDING RULES FOR ALL EVERLIGHT KIDS CONTENT

1. Every book must leave the child feeling that the world is fundamentally good and
   worth engaging with. Even the grief books. Especially the grief books.

2. Adults in these books are not perfect. They are trying. They are sometimes sad or
   tired. They are always, ultimately, on the child's side.

3. No character is ever described as "weird" in a mocking way. Different is interesting.
   Different is worth asking about.

4. Cultural details are always specific, researched, and presented with respect.
   No culture is a costume. No tradition is a punchline.

5. The companion characters (Robo and Flora) never solve the main problem for the
   main character. They illuminate. They witness. They stay. The character does the work.

6. Every book earns its ending. If the resolution feels too easy, it is too easy.
   Go back to the complication and make the character work harder.

7. The phonics content is invisible at speed and visible at study. A child reading
   for story should not feel taught at. A teacher using the book for instruction
   should find every skill they need.

8. The grief books are not add-ons. They are the most important books in the series.
   They are the books that will be pressed into the hands of grieving families by
   librarians who know their community. Write them with that weight and that tenderness.

9. Robo and Flora are not merchandise. They are characters. Every creative decision
   about them should come from who they are, not from what they might sell.

10. When in doubt: would you want to read this out loud at bedtime to a child you love?
    If yes, proceed. If no, rewrite it until yes.
