--The sun hovered low in the sky, casting long, amber shadows across the dusty streets of the
town. The heat had long since driven most people indoors, but even the shaded corners of the
wooden saloons and creaking porches offered little relief. The town felt quiet, too quiet. Hailey
could feel the tension in the air as if the whole place was holding its breath, waiting for
something unseen to happen.
She adjusted her hat against the glaring sun and squinted, surveying the nearly empty street
before her. A stray dog scurried into an alley, and a distant wind kicked up small whirlwinds of
dirt and debris that danced along the dry ground. It was a town like any other in the West,
decaying under the weight of time and hardship. The buildings that had once been painted
bright reds and blues were now chipped and faded, sun-beaten relics of a more prosperous era.
Hailey sighed, her hand brushing the badge pinned to her chest—a small token of authority that
weighed heavier with each passing day. She was twenty-two, wild at heart, full of ideas, and stubborn
as hell. But being a deputy in this town wasn’t easy. Every day, she fought against the
assumptions of the men around her, men who had long decided that her place was anywhere
but where she stood now.
She walked past the sheriff’s office, its windows cracked and clouded with dust, just like the man
who ran it. Inside, she knew the sheriff would be slouched back in his chair, half-asleep,
dismissive of the growing unrest in the town. The epidemic that had begun to creep into their
lives—slow, silent, and strange—wasn’t something he took seriously. He called it “hysteria,” “the
usual Western fever,” and waved her off every time she raised her concerns.
Hailey glanced over her shoulder as she passed the saloon. Its doors creaked open, and two
men shuffled out, shoulders slumped, eyes dull. There was something off about the way they
moved—like their bodies were heavy, too heavy. One of them stumbled, catching himself on a
railing, but neither said a word. Their faces were blank, drained of life. It was like they weren’t
really seeing the world around them.
The feeling of unease settled deeper in Hailey’s bones. She had been noticing more and more
people in the town moving that way. Slower, more lethargic, as if some unseen force was
sapping the energy right out of them. But it wasn’t enough to raise alarm yet, not for most. To
the others, it was just the heat, the hard life out West. Nothing new. But to Hailey, it felt different.
It felt wrong.
She continued walking down the street, past more empty storefronts and boarded-up windows.
It was late afternoon, but it could’ve been midnight for how still the town felt. Even the horses

tied up outside the general store stood silent, swatting their tails idly but otherwise motionless.
Something was coming. She could feel it.
As she reached the end of the street, Hailey turned toward her house. The sun beat down
harder now, reflecting off the cracked windowpane as she approached. She took a deep breath
before opening the door, her hand resting on the wooden handle for just a moment longer than
usual. Inside was another world, one that felt even more suffocating than the tense quiet of the
streets outside.
Her boyfriend, Jake, sat at the table, his eyes dark and narrowed. The bottle of whiskey on the
table was half-empty, and the air inside the house was thick, like the stale taste of regret and
anger mixed together.
“Where the hell have you been?” His voice was sharp, cutting through the silence like a knife.
“On patrol,” Hailey replied evenly, hanging her hat by the door. She kept her voice steady,
careful not to let the frustration she felt creep into her words.
Jake stood up, his chair scraping against the floor. “You’re always out. What’s there to patrol?
Ain’t no one doing anything in this godforsaken town.”
Hailey didn’t answer. She knew better than to escalate. He’d been drinking again, and that
always meant trouble. The lines of control in her life were blurring—just like the lines between
this world and the strange, otherworldly places she found herself in when she slept.
She moved quietly to the kitchen, her footsteps barely making a sound on the wooden
floorboards. She could feel Jake’s eyes on her, waiting, testing her patience.
“I’m trying to keep things together,” she muttered as she opened the pantry door. The emptiness
inside it reflected the hollowness of her words. There wasn’t much left—food was running low,
just like hope.
But Jake wasn’t one for sympathy. He grabbed her arm as she passed, his grip tight. “You ain’t
doing nothing but pretending you’re something you’re not. Deputy,” he spat the word like it
tasted bitter in his mouth. “You’re just playing dress-up.”
Hailey pulled her arm free, keeping her face neutral. Inside, a storm of anger brewed. She
wanted to scream, to fight back, but there was no point. Not tonight. Not in this house. She was
trapped here, just as much as she was trapped in this town.
“I’m going to bed,” she said quietly, walking away from the confrontation. She didn’t look back as
she closed the bedroom door behind her. Sleep couldn’t come fast enough. Because when she
slept, she left all of this behind. In sleep, she was free.

She lay down on the bed, staring at the cracked ceiling for a long time before finally closing her
eyes. The weight of the day’s tension melted away as she drifted into sleep, and soon, the
familiar sensation of lifting—of being unbound—washed over her.

world of the astral realm, guardians of this strange, beautiful dimension. Here, she wasn’t the

weight of the world had returned, but it was no longer tied to the physical world she had left. It

shadow across the sky, not fully formed but present all the same. The thrill she had felt

the safety of her body before whatever this was revealed itself.
But Hailey wasn’t one to run from fear. She narrowed her eyes, staring into the distance, trying
to focus on the shadow. She couldn’t make it out—only the vague impression of something
dark, something powerful, moving through the sky like a storm on the horizon.
The presence grew stronger, and the cold in the air deepened. Hailey’s instinct to leave was
fighting with her desire to stay, to confront whatever it was that lurked in this world with her. But
before she could decide, a gust of wind tore through the air, forcing her backward, spinning her
in the sky like a leaf caught in a violent storm. She lost control, tumbling through the air, the
wind howling in her ears.

dissipating. The shadow was gone, or at least, it had retreated. Hailey hovered in the sky, her

darkness that had just passed through.
It was gone, for now.

Hailey shuddered, shaking off the lingering fear. She wasn’t sure what that had been, but she
knew it wasn’t the last time she would encounter it. There was something in the astral realm with
her, something dark and powerful. She could feel it, like a distant storm on the edge of her
consciousness, waiting to strike again.
With a thought, she willed herself back toward the surface, letting the wind carry her gently
toward the sea. The creatures below continued their peaceful swim, unaware or unconcerned
with the shadow that had crossed the sky. Hailey wished she could be like them, carefree in this
beautiful world.
But she couldn’t shake the feeling that the shadow would return. And next time, it might not just
pass by.
