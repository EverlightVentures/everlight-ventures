# Library and School Distribution Checklist
## Adventures Series -- Self-Publisher Edition (2026)

Plain English. Do these in order. Do not skip Phase 1 -- everything downstream depends on it.

---

## PHASE 1: GET YOUR FOUNDATION RIGHT
*Before you approach a single library or school, these must be done.*

### ISBN and Copyright

- [ ] **Buy your own ISBNs from Bowker (myidentifiers.com).**
  - Cost: $125 for 10 ISBNs (best value), $295 for 100.
  - Do NOT use a free KDP-assigned ISBN. Libraries and distributors require publisher-owned ISBNs.
  - Assign one ISBN to hardcover, a separate one to softcover, a separate one to any ebook.
  - Set your own imprint name as the publisher (e.g., "Everlight Books" or whatever you want to be known as).

- [ ] **Register copyright with the U.S. Copyright Office (copyright.gov).**
  - Cost: $65 per book (online registration).
  - Not required to publish, but required before you can sue for infringement. Do it early.

### Library of Congress Control Number (LCCN)

- [ ] **Apply for a Preassigned Control Number (PCN) through the Library of Congress.**
  - Website: loc.gov/publish/pcn
  - Cost: Free.
  - You must apply BEFORE the book is printed. The LCCN goes on the copyright page.
  - Eligibility: U.S. publisher, book will be in libraries, hardcover or paperback (not ebook-only).
  - Allow 2-4 weeks for the number to be issued.
  - Why it matters: Libraries will not catalog a children's book without an LCCN. This is non-negotiable.

### Format

- [ ] **Print a hardcover edition.**
  - Libraries strongly prefer hardcover (it lasts 50+ checkouts). Paperback-only titles are routinely passed over by library selectors.
  - If you use IngramSpark, they offer caselaminate hardcover. That is the standard library finish.

### Returnable Policy

- [ ] **Set your title to "Returnable" in IngramSpark.**
  - Most libraries and school distributors will not order a title unless returns are allowed.
  - Yes, this means you can get books back. The trade-off is market access. Turn it on.
  - Discount setting: Set wholesale discount to 55% for libraries/schools (standard trade discount).

## PHASE 2: GET INTO THE DISTRIBUTION PIPELINE
*This is how your book gets visible to library buyers and school purchasers.*

### IngramSpark (Your Gateway -- Do This First)

- [ ] **Publish your hardcover on IngramSpark.**
  - Cost: $49 setup fee per title (sometimes waived during promotions).
  - IngramSpark feeds Follett Content and Mackin, which are the two primary school/library distributors as of 2026. You need to be in Ingram to be visible to them.
  - Make sure your title, ISBN, LCCN, author name, and series info are entered exactly right. This data propagates everywhere.

- [ ] **Confirm your title appears in the Ingram catalog.**
  - Log into IngramSpark and verify status shows "Available."
  - Allow 4-6 weeks after setup for data to flow to downstream buyers.

### Follett Content (Primary School Library Distributor)

- [ ] **Verify your title is discoverable in Follett's system.**
  - Follett Content is now the dominant distributor for K-12 school libraries and as of February 2026 has expanded to serve public libraries as well.
  - They pull data from Ingram. Your job is to make sure IngramSpark metadata is clean and complete (cover image, BISAC codes, age range, grade level, series name).
  - BISAC codes for children's picture books: JUV002000 (Concepts), JUV039000 (Social Themes), JUV013000 (Family), pick 2-3 that fit.

- [ ] **Direct outreach to Follett is optional but useful.**
  - If you have strong review coverage, you can email Follett's collection development team directly to flag your title. Include reviews and your educator's guide.

### Mackin (Secondary Distributor)

- [ ] **Mackin is the #2 school library distributor.**
  - Like Follett, Mackin pulls from Ingram. If your IngramSpark listing is clean, you should appear in their catalog automatically.
  - Mackin also has a "publisher direct" option. Visit mackin.com/publishers if you want to submit directly to supplement Ingram distribution.

## PHASE 3: GET REVIEWED
*Library selectors and school purchasing departments do not buy unreviewed children's books as a rule. Reviews are the unlock.*

Do these in priority order based on your budget.

- [ ] **School Library Journal -- Submit for free review.**
  - Website: slj.com (look for "Review Submissions" under "For Publishers")
  - Cost: Free.
  - SLJ reviews are the gold standard for school and public library acquisition. A positive review here will open more doors than anything else on this list.
  - Lead time: 4-6 months. Submit as early as possible, ideally with advance copies 3-4 months before publication date.
  - Tip: They prioritize hardcover submissions. Send physical copies.

- [ ] **Booklist -- Submit for free review.**
  - Published by the American Library Association (ALA). Also carries significant weight with public library selectors.
  - Cost: Free.
  - Submit physical copies via their publisher submission process (ala.org/booklist).
  - Lead time: Similar to SLJ, 3-5 months.

- [ ] **Publishers Weekly BookLife -- Paid review.**
  - Cost: $149 (standard) to $249 (expedited / more visibility).
  - Website: booklife.pub
  - Turnaround: 4-12 weeks depending on tier.
  - A BookLife review adds credibility when you are doing direct outreach to schools and libraries. Paste the pull quote in your pitch email.

- [ ] **Kirkus Reviews -- Paid, optional but prestigious.**
  - Cost: $399 per title.
  - Website: kirkusreviews.com
  - Worth it if your book is strong and you want the most recognizable name in early literacy circles. A starred Kirkus review for a children's book is a major marketing asset.
  - You choose whether to publish the review (you can suppress a bad one). Only publish if it is positive.

## PHASE 4: DIRECT SCHOOL OUTREACH
*Do not wait for schools to find you. Go to them.*

### Build Your Pitch Package

- [ ] **Assemble a one-page sell sheet per book.**
  - Cover image, book title, series name, ISBN, price, age range, CCSS standards met (from your educator's guide), CASEL competencies met, short summary, any review quotes, and your ordering info (IngramSpark / Follett / Mackin).

- [ ] **Attach the Educator's Guide.**
  - The CCSS and CASEL alignment table in your educator's guide is your primary credibility tool with schools. Curriculum directors respond to standards alignment. Lead with it.

- [ ] **Create a one-paragraph pitch email.**
  - Subject line example: "K-2 Picture Book Series | CCSS-Aligned | Educator's Guide Included"
  - Keep the email to 4-5 sentences. Attach the sell sheet and educator's guide as PDFs.

### Who to Contact at Schools

- [ ] **Elementary school principals and assistant principals.**
  - They often approve small purchases directly.

- [ ] **School librarians / library media specialists.**
  - These are your biggest allies. They make direct purchasing recommendations and often have independent budgets for new acquisitions.

- [ ] **Curriculum coordinators or instructional coaches at the district level.**
  - One yes from a curriculum coordinator can put your book into every classroom in a district.

- [ ] **Target Title I schools.**
  - Title I schools receive federal funding specifically for supplemental learning materials. They often have earmarked budgets for diverse, culturally relevant children's books and are actively looking for titles that hit CCSS and SEL standards.

### Where to Find Contacts

- [ ] Check district websites -- staff directories are usually public.
- [ ] School librarians are often reachable through the American Association of School Librarians (AASL) state chapter directories.
- [ ] Attend one local school board meeting or curriculum fair. In-person introductions convert far better than cold email.

## PHASE 5: PUBLIC LIBRARY OUTREACH

- [ ] **Contact your local library system's collection development librarian first.**
  - Ask them to add your title to their catalog. Local authors often get preferential treatment.
  - Offer to donate one copy and do a storytime read-aloud. A live event leads to catalog acquisition.

- [ ] **Offer author storytime visits (free is fine to start).**
  - Libraries that host your read-aloud are almost guaranteed to purchase a few copies for their collection afterward.
  - This also generates word of mouth and email list signups if you have a landing page.

- [ ] **Submit to state library recommended reading lists.**
  - Most state libraries publish annual recommended reading lists for children. Each state has its own submission process. Search "[your state] library children's recommended reading list submission."
