Throughout the entire rest of the manuscript (Ch 1-7, Ch 9 reflection scenes), the gambler is consistently "Tom Bricker." This is a clear error introduced in the heat of the battle chapter. Change "Tom Buckley" to "Tom Bricker."

- **Line 1280 (Ch 3, The Golden House) and Line 3520 (Ch 8):** The first victim girl is "Lily Morrison" throughout the main narrative (Ch 3). In the Ch 8 battle flash-empathy sequence she appears as "Lily Henderson." The Henderson family is a separate family in the book (the silent boy). "Lily Henderson" in line 3520 should be "Lily Morrison."

This is a careless greeting inconsistent with the established time of day. Change to "Afternoon, Sheriff" or simply remove the greeting and move directly to Hailey's report.

## Comma Artifact Scan (from dash replacement)

The 1,040-instance replacement of ` -- ` with `, ` was audited across the full manuscript. No double-comma artifacts (`, ,`) were found anywhere in the file. The grep returned zero matches for that pattern.

However, the replacement did produce a small number of structurally awkward comma constructions that read as run-on comma lists where an em dash would carry more rhetorical punch. These are not errors but are flagged in the Voice section below. No mechanical fix is required unless the author prefers to restore em dashes in selected spots.

One related note: the narrative voice occasionally uses ` -- ` in its remaining form (e.g., line 847: "She is almost --") for deliberate interruption effects, and these have correctly been left as-is. They are intentional and should not be changed.

## Voice and Flow Notes

These are observations, not corrections. The author should weigh them against their own intentions. Nothing here is broken; these are places where a second pass might deepen the work.

**1. The badge-touching reflex is overused as a grounding mechanism.**
Hailey presses her thumb against her badge at lines 234, 398, 516, 635, 755, 781, 1029, 1120, 2166, and others. It is a strong character anchor the first several times and earns its place. By the midpoint of the book, readers may begin to clock it as automatic rather than meaningful. The author may want to audit the total count and thin it in Chapters 4 and 5, where it clusters. Preserve the most emotionally loaded instances (the first time in Ch 1, and the final phantom-badge moment in Ch 10 where there is no badge to touch).

**2. "One thing at a time" appears as a chapter-closing beat twice in Chapter 1 (lines 406 and 518).**
Both occurrences are within roughly 100 lines of each other, in the same chapter, used as the same kind of grounding statement. This reads as a duplication rather than a deliberate echo. The first instance (line 406) may be the stronger placement. The second (line 518) lands just before the Jonah encounter and may benefit from a different closing beat that raises the tension rather than settling it.

**3. Water/ocean simile density in the Prologue and early astral sequences.**
The quantum web "receives her the way water receives ink" appears in the Prologue (line 185) and is reprised almost identically in Ch 8 (line 3666: "absorb her the way water absorbs a drop of ink") and again in Ch 9 (line 4403: "receive her the way water receives ink"). This is clearly an intentional structural callback, and as a bookending device it works. The Ch 9 repetition, in particular, landing immediately after the sacrifice, will resonate with readers who remember the Prologue. No change needed -- this is a feature, flagged only so the author confirms it is deliberate.

**4. The Widow Harlan street address shifts.**
She is referred to as living on "Tanner Street" (early chapters), then "Maple Street" (Ch 3, line 1132), then "Elm Street" (Ch 8, line 3520). The first two are plausible if she moved between scenes, but a character of her type (isolated, static, refusing connection) would not move. Recommend verifying which street the author intends and applying it consistently. This is a borderline continuity error but may be intentional local flavor. Flagged for author review.

**5. Jake's backstory detail introduced late, slightly jarring.**
Line 1659 (Ch 4) describes Jake as an "ex-army flunkout who had been discharged for a temper he could never keep leashed." This is specific backstory that arrives in Chapter 4, well into the reader's understanding of Jake as a domestic abuser. The characterization itself is effective; the timing means readers have formed their picture of Jake without it. This is a structural note, not a flaw -- but if the author wants this detail to shape reader sympathy earlier, it could be seeded in the Ch 1 or Ch 2 Jake scenes. No action required unless the author agrees.
